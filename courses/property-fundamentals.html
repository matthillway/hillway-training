What is Commercial Property?

Module 1

The Big Picture

Commercial property (also called commercial real estate or CRE) is any property used primarily for business purposes rather than residential living. In the UK, the commercial property market is worth over £1 trillion and is a major asset class alongside equities (stocks) and bonds. As a surveyor, you will be advising clients on how to buy, sell, lease, manage and value these assets.

The term "property" in surveying always means real property: land and buildings. It is a physical asset you can visit and inspect, which makes it fundamentally different from financial instruments like shares or bonds. This physicality is both its strength (you can see and touch it) and its weakness (it takes time to buy and sell).

Types of Commercial Property

🏢

Offices

Spaces where businesses operate. Classified by grade (A, B, C) reflecting quality, age and specification. City centre, out-of-town business parks, serviced offices and co-working spaces all fall under this category.

🛒

Retail

Shops, shopping centres, retail parks and supermarkets. The retail sector has changed dramatically with the rise of e-commerce. High street, out-of-town, convenience and bulky goods retail are distinct sub-markets.

🏭

Industrial & Logistics

Warehouses, distribution centres, manufacturing units and trade counters. This sector has boomed due to online shopping. "Last mile" delivery hubs near cities are especially in demand.

🏘

Mixed-Use

Developments combining two or more uses, typically retail at ground floor with offices or residential above. Common in town centres and increasingly popular with planners and investors.

📈

Alternatives

A growing category including hotels, student accommodation, healthcare, data centres, self-storage and leisure. Often called "operational real estate" because the property and the business within it are closely linked.

🏞

Land & Development

Undeveloped or under-utilised sites with potential for building. Value depends on planning permission status, location and market demand. Development appraisal is a specialist skill within surveying.

Freehold vs Leasehold

These are the two fundamental ways to hold an interest in property. Understanding the difference is critical for everything you will do as a surveyor.

Freehold Leasehold
You own the building and the land it sits on, forever You have the right to occupy the building for a set period
Also called "fee simple" or "freehold absolute" Created by a lease (a contract) between landlord and tenant
Maximum control over the property Subject to terms and restrictions in the lease
Responsible for all maintenance and insurance Obligations depend on what the lease says
Can grant leases to tenants Can sometimes sublet (if the lease allows)
Valued by investment yield / capital value Valued by rent passing vs market rent and unexpired term

In practice, most commercial property is held as an investment by a landlord (freeholder) who grants leases to occupying businesses (tenants). The landlord earns rental income; the tenant gets a place to operate.

How Property Differs from Other Asset Classes

Your business degree will have covered equities and bonds. Property sits alongside these but has unique characteristics:

  • Illiquid — You cannot sell a building in seconds like you can sell shares. Transactions take weeks or months.
  • Heterogeneous — Every property is unique. Two identical-looking buildings on the same street can have very different values because of lease terms, condition or tenant quality.
  • High transaction costs — Legal fees, stamp duty land tax (SDLT), agent fees and surveys make buying and selling expensive.
  • Physical asset — It needs maintaining. Roofs leak, boilers break, tenants cause damage. This is why surveyors exist.
  • Income-producing — Like bonds, property generates regular income (rent). Unlike bonds, you can actively manage and improve the asset to increase returns.
  • Leveraged — Most property is bought with a combination of equity and debt (mortgage). This amplifies returns but also risk.
  • Location-dependent — A shop on a busy high street is worth more than an identical shop on a quiet side road. Location is the single biggest driver of value.

Key Terminology

You will encounter these terms constantly. Learn them now and you will be ahead from day one at CEM.

Yield
The annual rental income expressed as a percentage of the property's capital value. A 6% yield means a £1m property produces £60,000 per year rent. Lower yields = higher quality/lower risk assets.
ERV (Estimated Rental Value)
What a property would let for on the open market today. Used in valuations, rent reviews and investment analysis. Sometimes called Market Rent.
Void
A period when a property is empty and producing no rental income. Void periods cost the landlord money (lost rent, rates liability, security, maintenance).
Covenant Strength
How financially reliable a tenant is. A blue-chip tenant like Tesco has a strong covenant. A start-up has a weak covenant. Stronger covenant = lower risk = lower yield.
Dilapidations
The disrepair a tenant is liable to remedy under the terms of their lease, usually assessed at lease end. A major area of surveying work.
WAULT
Weighted Average Unexpired Lease Term. A measure of how long, on average, tenants are committed to staying. Longer WAULT = more secure income = more valuable.
Cap Rate / Capitalisation Rate
Same concept as yield but more commonly used internationally. The rate at which net operating income is capitalised to determine property value.
Prime vs Secondary
Prime properties are the best quality in the best locations with the strongest tenants. Secondary is everything below that. The gap in value between the two is called the "prime-secondary spread."
Head Lease / Underlease
The head lease is between the freeholder and first tenant. An underlease (sublease) is granted by that tenant to a sub-tenant. Multiple layers of leases can exist on one property.
FRI Lease
Full Repairing and Insuring lease. The tenant pays for all repairs and insurance. This is the standard for single-let commercial properties. The landlord receives "clean" net income.
Q

Module 1 Knowledge Check

Question 1 of 5
A property investor tells you a warehouse has a yield of 5%. What does this mean?
Correct. Yield is the relationship between annual rent and capital value. A £2 million warehouse at 5% yield produces £100,000 per annum in rent. As a general rule, lower yields indicate lower risk (prime) assets, and higher yields indicate higher risk (secondary) assets.
Question 2 of 5
What is the key difference between freehold and leasehold ownership?
Freehold gives you outright ownership of the land and building in perpetuity. A leasehold is a time-limited right to occupy, created by a lease contract between the freeholder (landlord) and the leaseholder (tenant). Both freehold and leasehold exist in residential and commercial property.
Question 3 of 5
Which sector of commercial property has seen the most growth due to online shopping?
E-commerce has driven massive demand for warehouses and distribution centres, especially "last mile" logistics hubs near cities. Meanwhile, high street retail has been negatively affected by the same trend, with many shops closing as spending moves online.
Question 4 of 5
A property has a strong tenant covenant. What impact does this have on yield?
Investors pay more for secure income. If a tenant like the Government or a major bank is unlikely to default, investors accept a lower return (yield) because the risk is lower. A weak covenant tenant (say a new start-up) means higher risk, so investors demand a higher yield to compensate. Yield and risk move together.
Question 5 of 5
What does "dilapidations" mean in commercial property?
Dilapidations is a major area of surveying practice. When a lease ends, the landlord assesses what condition the tenant should return the property in (based on the lease covenants) versus its actual condition. The tenant may need to carry out repairs, or more commonly, pay compensation. This process is governed by the Dilapidations Protocol published by the Property Litigation Association.

How Commercial Property Works

Module 2

The Landlord and Tenant Relationship

At the heart of commercial property is the relationship between landlord and tenant. The landlord owns the building and wants to maximise rental income while protecting the value of their asset. The tenant needs a space to operate their business and wants flexibility, certainty and reasonable costs. These interests sometimes align and sometimes conflict. As a surveyor, you will often act for one side or the other, and understanding both perspectives is essential.

This relationship is governed by one document above all others: the lease.

The Lease: Key Terms You Must Know

A commercial lease is a legally binding contract that defines the relationship between landlord and tenant. Leases can run from a few pages to several hundred. Here are the terms that matter most:

Lease Term What It Means
Term The length of the lease. Typical commercial leases run 5, 10 or 15 years. The trend is towards shorter terms as tenants want more flexibility.
Rent The annual amount the tenant pays, usually quarterly in advance on standard quarter days (25 March, 24 June, 29 September, 25 December). Increasingly paid monthly.
Service Charge The tenant's share of the costs of maintaining shared areas and services (cleaning, security, lifts, common repairs). Applies mainly to multi-let buildings.
Insurance Rent The tenant's contribution to the building insurance premium. The landlord insures the building and recovers the cost from tenants.
Break Clause An option to end the lease early, usually at a specific date (e.g., year 5 of a 10-year lease). Can be tenant-only, landlord-only, or mutual. Often subject to strict conditions.
Rent Review A mechanism to adjust the rent during the lease term, typically every 5 years. Ensures rent keeps pace with the market. Several types exist (see below).
Repairing Obligation Who pays for repairs. FRI (Full Repairing and Insuring) puts it all on the tenant. Internal Repairing only covers the inside. The lease wording is critical.
Alienation Whether the tenant can transfer the lease (assignment) or sublet part or all of the property. Most leases allow this with the landlord's consent, which cannot be unreasonably withheld.
User Clause Restricts what the property can be used for. A lease might permit "offices only" or "retail Class E." Changing use usually needs landlord consent and may need planning permission.
Alterations Whether the tenant can modify the property. Usually split into structural (rarely permitted) and non-structural (usually permitted with consent). A licence to alter is needed.

Rent Review Mechanisms

Rent reviews are one of the most common tasks for a commercial surveyor. There are three main types:

Open Market Rent Review

The most common type. The rent is adjusted to whatever the property would let for on the open market at the review date. The surveyor considers comparable evidence (what similar properties have let for nearby). If landlord and tenant cannot agree, the matter goes to an independent expert or arbitrator. These reviews are almost always "upward only," meaning the rent can only stay the same or increase, never decrease.

RPI/CPI Linked (Index-Linked)

The rent increases in line with inflation, measured by the Retail Price Index (RPI) or Consumer Price Index (CPI). Simple and predictable, but the rent can drift away from actual market levels over time. Common in sale-and-leaseback transactions and long leases.

Fixed Uplift

The rent increases by a predetermined amount or percentage at set dates. For example, £50,000 per annum in years 1-5, £55,000 in years 6-10. Provides certainty for both parties but does not reflect market conditions.

Service Charges Explained

When a building has multiple tenants sharing common areas (corridors, car parks, reception, lifts), the landlord provides services and recovers the cost through a service charge. This is a significant cost for tenants and a major source of disputes.

  • Budget — At the start of each service charge year, the landlord prepares a budget of expected costs and collects payments on account from tenants.
  • Apportionment — Each tenant pays a proportion, usually based on floor area. A tenant occupying 20% of the building pays 20% of the service charge.
  • Reconciliation — At year end, actual costs are compared to the budget. If the landlord spent more, tenants pay a balancing charge. If less, they receive a credit.
  • RICS Code — The RICS Professional Statement on Service Charges in Commercial Property sets out best practice. It requires transparency, reasonableness and proper certification of accounts. Surveyors are expected to comply.
  • Common costs — Cleaning, security, lift maintenance, building management fees, communal repairs, landscaping, utilities for common areas and building insurance.

Business Rates

Business rates are a tax on commercial property, similar to council tax for homes. They are a major occupational cost that tenants must understand.

  • Rateable Value (RV) — Set by the Valuation Office Agency (VOA), this represents the annual open market rental value of the property at a set valuation date. The current rating list is based on values at 1 April 2023.
  • Multiplier (UBR) — The government sets a pence-in-the-pound figure (the Uniform Business Rate) that is applied to the rateable value. For 2025/26, the standard multiplier is approximately 55.5p.
  • Calculation — Rates payable = Rateable Value x Multiplier. A property with an RV of £100,000 pays approximately £55,500 per year in rates.
  • Empty rates — Landlords are liable for business rates on empty properties after an initial void period (3 months for offices/retail, 6 months for industrial). This is a significant cost of vacancy.
  • Appeals — If a ratepayer believes the rateable value is too high, they can challenge it through the VOA's Check, Challenge, Appeal process. Rating surveyors specialise in this.

Insurance

Property insurance is a critical but often overlooked aspect of commercial property. The key distinctions are:

  • Building insurance — Covers the structure of the building against damage (fire, flood, storm, subsidence). The landlord usually arranges this and recovers the cost from tenants as insurance rent. The sum insured should reflect the full reinstatement cost (cost to rebuild), not the market value.
  • Contents insurance — Covers the tenant's own fixtures, fittings, stock and equipment. This is the tenant's responsibility and is separate from the building policy.
  • Loss of rent insurance — Covers the landlord's rental income if the building is damaged and cannot be occupied. Typically covers 3 years' rent.
  • Public liability — Covers claims from third parties injured on the property. Both landlord and tenant need their own policies.

The lease defines who insures what. Getting this wrong can leave a building uninsured or double-insured, both of which are problems.

A lease is the DNA of a commercial property. Everything a surveyor does, from rent reviews to dilapidations to service charges, starts with reading and understanding the lease. Get comfortable reading legal documents. It is a skill that will define your career.

Module 2 Knowledge Check

Question 1 of 5
A tenant has a 10-year lease with a break clause at year 5. What does this mean?
A break clause gives the right (but not the obligation) to terminate the lease early at a specified date. Whether it is a tenant break, landlord break, or mutual break depends on the lease wording. Break clauses often have strict conditions (e.g., all rent must be paid, the property must be in good repair) that must be met precisely, or the break is invalid.
Question 2 of 5
An open market rent review is "upward only." What does this mean?
In an upward only rent review, if the market rent has fallen below the current passing rent, the rent stays the same (a "standstill"). The rent can never be reduced below the previous level. This protects the landlord's income and the investment value of the property. Tenants and their advisors have long argued against upward only reviews, but they remain the market standard.
Question 3 of 5
Which document sets out best practice for service charges in commercial property?
The RICS Professional Statement on Service Charges in Commercial Property (formerly the RICS Service Charge Code) is mandatory for RICS members. It covers transparency, timeliness of accounts, reasonableness of expenditure, and the rights of tenants to challenge costs. The Red Book covers valuations, not service charges. The 1954 Act covers security of tenure.
Question 4 of 5
A commercial property has a rateable value of £80,000 and the multiplier is 55p. What are the approximate annual business rates?
Business rates = Rateable Value x Multiplier. £80,000 x 0.55 = £44,000. This is a significant annual cost for tenants and must be factored into total occupational costs alongside rent, service charge and insurance.
Question 5 of 5
What should building insurance be based on?
Building insurance should be based on the Declared Value / Day One Reinstatement Cost, which is the cost to completely rebuild the property from scratch, including demolition, site clearance, professional fees and an allowance for inflation during the rebuild period. This is different from (and usually lower than) the market value, which includes land value and other factors.

The Surveying Profession

Module 3

What is RICS?

The Royal Institution of Chartered Surveyors (RICS) is the global professional body for surveyors. Founded in 1868 and headquartered in London, it sets and enforces standards for the profession worldwide. Being "RICS regulated" means Hillway operates under the highest professional standards in the industry.

RICS is not a trade body or a union. It is a regulatory body with the power to discipline, fine and expel members. When a firm displays the RICS lion logo, clients know they are dealing with a firm that has professional indemnity insurance, complaints procedures, and regulated conduct.

  • 180,000+ members worldwide across 146 countries
  • Designation letters: MRICS (Member), FRICS (Fellow), AssocRICS (Associate)
  • Regulated firms must follow RICS Rules of Conduct and hold PI insurance
  • Standards: The RICS publishes professional standards, guidance notes and practice statements that members must follow

RICS Ethical Standards

RICS members must uphold five professional and ethical standards. These are not optional guidelines; they are rules. Breaching them can lead to formal disciplinary action.

1
Act with integrity

Be honest and straightforward in all you do. Never mislead a client or colleague.

2
Always provide a high standard of service

Competent, diligent, timely work. If you are not qualified to do something, say so.

3
Act in a way that promotes trust in the profession

Your behaviour reflects on all surveyors. Maintain professional conduct at all times.

4
Treat others with respect

No discrimination, bullying or harassment. Work collaboratively and respectfully.

5
Take responsibility

Be accountable for your actions. If you make a mistake, own it and fix it.

Types of Surveyor

Surveying is a broad profession with many specialisms. Here are the main types you will encounter:

Commercial Property Surveyor

Advises on the letting, sale, purchase, management and valuation of offices, shops, warehouses and other commercial buildings. This is what you are training to become.

Building Surveyor

Specialises in the physical condition of buildings. Carries out surveys, advises on defects, manages construction projects, handles party wall matters and dilapidations.

Residential Surveyor

Deals with houses and flats. Carries out mortgage valuations, homebuyer surveys and building surveys for domestic properties. Often works for lenders.

Quantity Surveyor (QS)

Manages costs on construction projects. Prepares budgets, tenders, valuations and final accounts. Works closely with contractors and developers.

Planning Consultant

Advises on the planning system, submits planning applications and represents clients at planning inquiries. Understanding what can be built where is key to development value.

Valuation Surveyor

Provides formal valuations of property for lending, accounts, taxation, dispute resolution and investment purposes. Must comply with the RICS Valuation Standards ("Red Book").

Property Management in Practice

Module 4

What is Property Management?

Property management is the day-to-day operation of commercial real estate on behalf of the landlord. It is where the theory of Modules 1-3 meets real life. A property manager is the person who makes sure the building runs smoothly, the tenants are looked after, the income is collected, and the asset retains its value. At Hillway, property management is a core service and you will be involved from your first week.

Good property management is proactive, not reactive. You do not wait for problems to find you. You anticipate them, plan for them, and deal with them before they become crises.

Day-to-Day Property Management Tasks

Here are the core activities that make up property management work:

  • Rent collection and credit control — Ensuring tenants pay rent on time. Chasing arrears promptly. Maintaining accurate records of all payments received.
  • Lease compliance — Monitoring whether tenants are complying with their lease obligations (using the property correctly, maintaining it, not making unauthorised alterations).
  • Landlord compliance — Ensuring the landlord meets their obligations too (maintaining common areas, providing services, insuring the building).
  • Contractor management — Appointing and supervising cleaning companies, security firms, maintenance contractors and managing agents. Getting best value for money.
  • Tenant communication — Being the point of contact for tenant queries, complaints and requests. Good tenant relationships reduce voids and disputes.
  • Financial reporting — Preparing management accounts, service charge budgets and reconciliations, rent schedules and cash flow forecasts for the landlord.
  • Insurance administration — Ensuring policies are renewed, premiums are recovered from tenants, and claims are processed when damage occurs.

Tenant Liaison and Rent Collection

Rent is the lifeblood of a property investment. Collecting it efficiently and managing arrears professionally is fundamental.

The Rent Collection Cycle

  • Demand — Issue rent demands to tenants before each quarter day (or monthly, depending on the lease). The demand must correctly state the amount, the period and the payment details.
  • Collection — Monitor bank accounts for incoming payments. Match payments to invoices. Most tenants now pay by BACS transfer.
  • Arrears management — If rent is not received within 7 days, send a formal reminder. Escalate to phone call at 14 days. Formal letter (Letter Before Action) at 21 days. Consider legal remedies (CRAR, forfeiture, statutory demand) if the tenant does not engage.
  • Reporting — Provide the landlord with a rent collection report showing amounts due, amounts received and any arrears. Typically monthly.

CRAR: Commercial Rent Arrears Recovery

CRAR replaced the ancient remedy of "distress" in 2014. It allows a landlord to instruct a certified enforcement agent (bailiff) to enter the premises and seize the tenant's goods to the value of the rent owed. It can only be used for principal rent (not service charges or insurance) and requires 7 days' written notice. It is a powerful tool but must be used carefully.

Service Charge Budgeting and Reconciliation

As you learned in Module 2, multi-let buildings recover running costs through a service charge. Managing this process is a significant part of property management work.

  • Budget preparation — Before each service charge year, prepare a detailed budget by cost category (cleaning, security, utilities, repairs, management fees, insurance). Base it on prior year actuals plus known changes.
  • On-account collection — Collect payments from tenants (usually quarterly) based on the budget. Each tenant pays their apportioned share.
  • Cost management — Throughout the year, review contractor invoices, challenge unreasonable costs, ensure works are completed to a satisfactory standard and authorise payments.
  • Year-end reconciliation — After the service charge year ends, prepare a detailed reconciliation comparing actual expenditure to budget. Issue a balancing charge or credit to each tenant.
  • Certification — The RICS Code requires service charge accounts to be certified (audited) by a qualified accountant if the total expenditure exceeds a specified threshold.
  • Tenant queries — Tenants have the right to inspect supporting documentation. Be prepared to justify every cost. Transparency builds trust and reduces disputes.

Property Inspections and Schedules of Condition

Regular physical inspections are essential for managing a property properly. You need to know what state the building is in at all times.

Routine Inspections

Visit the property at regular intervals (quarterly for multi-let, at least annually for single-let). Walk the common areas, check for defects, note any unauthorised works by tenants, inspect safety equipment (fire exits, extinguishers, emergency lighting). Take photographs. Write up a formal report with action items and costs.

Schedules of Condition

A Schedule of Condition is a photographic and written record of the state of a property at a specific point in time. It is most commonly prepared at the start of a lease to record what condition the property was in when the tenant took occupation. This protects the tenant from being held liable for pre-existing defects at lease end (dilapidations). A well-prepared schedule of condition is an essential piece of evidence and a core surveying skill.

Health and Safety Compliance

The landlord (or managing agent acting on their behalf) has legal obligations to ensure the safety of everyone who uses the building. This includes tenants, their staff, visitors and contractors. Non-compliance can result in criminal prosecution.

  • Fire Risk Assessment — Required under the Regulatory Reform (Fire Safety) Order 2005. Must be carried out by a competent person and reviewed regularly. The "responsible person" (usually the landlord for common areas) must implement recommendations.
  • Asbestos management — Buildings constructed before 2000 may contain asbestos. The landlord must maintain an asbestos management plan and ensure anyone working in the building is aware of its location. Do not disturb materials that may contain asbestos.
  • Legionella risk assessment — Required for water systems that could harbour legionella bacteria (cooling towers, hot water systems, spa pools). Regular monitoring and testing is essential.
  • Electrical testing — Fixed wiring inspections (EICR) should be carried out every 5 years. PAT testing for portable appliances. Ensure emergency lighting and fire alarm systems are tested regularly.
  • Lift inspections — Passenger lifts must be inspected every 6 months by a competent engineer under the Lifting Operations and Lifting Equipment Regulations (LOLER).
  • Risk assessments — General risk assessments for common areas covering slips, trips, falls, working at height and lone working.

Lease Events Diary Management

A lease events diary is the single most important tool in property management. Missing a critical date can cost tens or hundreds of thousands of pounds.

Event Why It Matters Lead Time
Rent review date Must serve notice on time to trigger the review. Missing the date can mean losing the right to increase rent for years. 6-12 months before
Break date If acting for a tenant exercising a break, notice must be served precisely on time and conditions met exactly. One day late = break is lost. 6-12 months before
Lease expiry Plan for re-letting, rent negotiations or tenant departure. The Landlord and Tenant Act 1954 may give the tenant the right to a new lease. 12-18 months before
Section 25 / Section 26 notices Formal notices under the 1954 Act. The landlord serves a Section 25 notice to end the tenancy. The tenant serves a Section 26 request for a new tenancy. 6-12 months before expiry
Insurance renewal Building insurance must never lapse. Renew before expiry and update declared values. 1-2 months before
Service charge year end Trigger reconciliation process, instruct accountant for certification, prepare next year's budget. Immediately after year end

At Hillway, we maintain a digital lease events diary with automated reminders. Every critical date has a cascade of alerts: 12 months, 6 months, 3 months and 1 month before the event. You will be responsible for maintaining this diary for your allocated properties.

Property management is not glamorous work, but it is where you learn the most. Every other area of surveying, from agency to valuation to development, benefits from understanding how a building actually runs day to day. The best surveyors I know all started in management.

Question 1 of 5
What is CRAR in the context of rent arrears?
CRAR (Commercial Rent Arrears Recovery) replaced the ancient remedy of "distress" under the Tribunals, Courts and Enforcement Act 2007. It allows a landlord to instruct a certified enforcement agent to seize tenant goods to the value of unpaid principal rent. Key requirements: 7 days' written notice, only for principal rent (not service charge or insurance), minimum 7 days' arrears, and only certified agents can be used.
Question 2 of 5
What is a Schedule of Condition used for?
A Schedule of Condition is a photographic and written record of a property's state at a specific date, most commonly at lease commencement. It limits the tenant's repairing liability by documenting what condition the property was in when they took it. Without one, a tenant on an FRI lease could be liable to return the property in better condition than they found it.
Question 3 of 5
Why is missing a break clause date potentially catastrophic for a tenant?
Break clauses are strictly interpreted by the courts. If the notice is served one day late, or if a condition of the break (e.g., all rent paid, property in repair) is not precisely met, the break is invalid and the lease continues. A tenant on a 10-year lease who misses a year-5 break could be liable for 5 more years of rent. This is why the lease events diary is so critical.
Question 4 of 5
Under fire safety regulations, who is typically the "responsible person" for common areas in a multi-let commercial building?
Under the Regulatory Reform (Fire Safety) Order 2005, the "responsible person" for common areas is the landlord or whoever has control of the premises. In practice, this responsibility is often delegated to a managing agent (like Hillway) who carries out fire risk assessments, ensures fire safety equipment is maintained and that evacuation procedures are in place. Tenants are responsible for their own demised areas.
Question 5 of 5
What is the purpose of service charge reconciliation at year end?
During the service charge year, tenants pay on-account based on the budget. At year end, the managing agent compares what was actually spent to what was budgeted. If actual costs were higher, tenants pay a balancing charge. If lower, they receive a credit. The RICS Professional Statement requires this to be done within set timeframes and, above certain thresholds, the accounts must be independently certified by a qualified accountant.

What to Study Next

Before CEM starts on 30 March, consider reading around these topics to give yourself an advantage:

  • The Landlord and Tenant Act 1954 — The most important piece of legislation in commercial property. It gives business tenants "security of tenure" (the right to a new lease when the current one expires). Understanding this Act is fundamental. Start with the RICS guidance note on the topic.
  • RICS Valuation Standards (Red Book) — Browse the key definitions and valuation bases (Market Value, Market Rent, Fair Value). You do not need to memorise it, but knowing it exists and what it covers will help.
  • Estates Gazette (EG) — The trade publication for commercial property. Read it weekly to stay current with market trends, transactions and developments. Available online at eg.co.uk.
  • Property Week — Another key trade publication. Between EG and Property Week, you will have comprehensive market coverage.
  • Measurement standards — RICS has adopted IPMS (International Property Measurement Standards) for measuring buildings. Understanding NIA (Net Internal Area), GIA (Gross Internal Area) and GEA (Gross External Area) is essential.
  • Comparable evidence — Practice looking at property transaction data. CoStar, EGi and the VOA website are key sources. Understanding how to analyse and apply comparable evidence is a core surveying skill.
