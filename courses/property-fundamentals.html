Day 1: Introduction to Commercial Property

Understanding the fundamentals — what commercial property is, how it works, and the language of the industry.

1

What is Commercial Property?

Day 1

Definition

Commercial property (also called commercial real estate or CRE) is any property used for business purposes rather than as a home. It is a distinct asset class — alongside equities, bonds, and cash — and forms a significant part of the UK economy.

The UK commercial property market is worth approximately £1 trillion. Every business in the country operates from some form of property, whether that is an office in a city centre, a warehouse on an industrial estate, or a shop on the high street.

Why Does Commercial Property Matter?

  • Economic backbone: Property provides the physical infrastructure for every business. Without offices, warehouses, shops, and factories, economic activity cannot happen.
  • Employment: The property industry directly employs over 500,000 people in the UK, from surveyors and architects to construction workers and property managers.
  • Investment returns: Commercial property has historically delivered total returns of 7–10% per annum over the long term, combining rental income with capital growth.
  • Pension funds: Your pension fund almost certainly holds commercial property. Major institutional investors typically allocate 5–15% of their portfolios to real estate.
  • Government revenue: Business rates, stamp duty land tax (SDLT), and other property taxes generate billions in government revenue annually.

Commercial vs Residential Property

Every building you walk past has a story — who owns it, who occupies it, what rent they pay, when the lease expires, and what it might be worth. As a surveyor, your job is to understand all of those things.

Types of Commercial Property

Day 1

Commercial property is divided into several main sectors. Each has its own market dynamics, lease structures, and valuation considerations. You will encounter all of them during your career.

🏢

Offices

Headquarters, serviced offices, business parks, science parks. The UK office market is centred on London (the City, West End, Docklands) but every major city has a significant office market. Sheffield’s office core is around the city centre and Kelham Island.

🛒

Retail

High street shops, shopping centres, retail warehouses, supermarkets, convenience stores. Retail has been reshaped by e-commerce — online sales now account for approximately 27% of all retail spending in the UK.

🏭

Industrial

Factories, workshops, trade counters, and light industrial units. Traditional industrial property is being supplemented and in some cases replaced by logistics and distribution facilities.

🚚

Logistics & Distribution

Large distribution warehouses (“big box” — typically 100,000+ sq ft), last-mile delivery hubs, and urban logistics facilities. This sector has seen enormous growth driven by e-commerce. Amazon, Ocado, and major retailers are key occupiers.

🏘

Alternatives

Student accommodation, healthcare (care homes, hospitals), hotels, data centres, self-storage, car parks, and leisure facilities. “Alternatives” have grown significantly as investors seek diversification and higher yields.

🏗

Mixed-Use

Developments combining multiple uses — typically retail on the ground floor with offices or residential above. Sheffield’s Heart of the City II is a major mixed-use regeneration scheme combining offices, retail, leisure, and public realm.

How Property is Measured

Commercial property is measured in square feet (sq ft) in the UK, although square metres (sq m) are sometimes used, particularly in academic contexts. The standard measurement basis is defined by the RICS Code of Measuring Practice (6th Edition) and more recently the IPMS (International Property Measurement Standards).

  • GIA (Gross Internal Area): Measured to the internal face of the external walls. Used for industrial/warehouse property and building costs.
  • NIA (Net Internal Area): The usable floor area, excluding common parts, toilets, lifts, and structural walls. Used for offices and retail.
  • GEA (Gross External Area): Measured to the external face of the external walls. Used for planning and some rating purposes.

Property as an Asset Class

Day 1

The Four Main Asset Classes

Investors allocate capital across four main asset classes. Understanding how property compares is fundamental to understanding why people invest in it.

Asset ClassReturn DriverLiquidityTypical Allocation
Equities (Shares)Dividends + capital growthHigh (traded daily)40–60%
Bonds (Gilts/Corporate)Coupon paymentsHigh20–40%
Property (Real Estate)Rent + capital growthLow (months to sell)5–15%
CashInterestHighest5–10%

Why Invest in Property?

  • Income yield: Property provides a reliable, contracted income stream through rent. A well-let office building might produce a yield of 5–7% per annum — significantly more than gilts or cash deposits.
  • Inflation hedge: Rents tend to rise with inflation (or above it), particularly where rent reviews are upward-only. This makes property attractive in inflationary environments.
  • Tangible asset: Unlike shares or bonds, property is a physical thing you can visit, manage, and improve. This appeals to many investors psychologically.
  • Value-add potential: You can increase the value of property through active management — refurbishment, re-letting at higher rents, change of use, or redevelopment.
  • Leverage: Banks will lend against property (typically 50–70% loan-to-value), allowing investors to amplify their returns through debt.
  • Diversification: Property returns have a low correlation with equities, so holding property in a portfolio reduces overall volatility.

The Downsides of Property Investment

  • Illiquidity: You cannot sell a building in a day. A typical sale takes 3–6 months (or longer in a downturn). This is property’s biggest disadvantage compared to equities and bonds.
  • Lot size: Individual properties are expensive. Even a modest industrial unit might cost £500,000+. This limits diversification for smaller investors.
  • Management burden: Property needs active management — maintenance, tenant relationships, lease events, compliance. It is not a “set and forget” investment.
  • Depreciation: Buildings physically deteriorate over time and can become obsolete (e.g., older offices without air conditioning or modern IT infrastructure).
  • Void risk: When a tenant leaves, the landlord receives no rent but still pays insurance, empty rates, and maintenance costs.
  • Valuation lag: Property values are based on appraisals, not daily market transactions. This means values can appear stable when they are actually falling.

Total Return = Income Return + Capital Growth

Property returns are measured as “total return”, which combines two elements:

  • Income return: The annual rental income expressed as a percentage of capital value (i.e., the yield).
  • Capital growth: The change in the property’s capital value over the period.

Historically, income has been the dominant component, accounting for roughly 60–70% of total return. This makes property more “bond-like” than “equity-like” in character, although capital values can be volatile in the short term.

The Property Market Cycle

Day 1

Understanding the Cycle

Property markets move in cycles. Understanding where you are in the cycle is one of the most important skills in property — it determines whether it is a good time to buy, sell, develop, or hold.

The typical property cycle lasts 7–10 years from peak to peak, though cycles can be shorter or longer depending on economic conditions, interest rates, and external shocks.

The Four Phases

PhaseCharacteristicsWhat Happens
1. Recovery Rising take-up, falling vacancy, limited new supply Rents stabilise after falling. Investor confidence returns. Bargain-hunters enter the market. Development activity is minimal because the previous downturn killed pipeline.
2. Growth / Expansion Strong demand, rising rents, new development starts Rents rise. Yields compress (values increase). Developers start new schemes. Bank lending loosens. Optimism builds. Investment volumes increase.
3. Peak / Boom Oversupply building, speculative development, aggressive pricing Rents may still be rising but at a slowing rate. New supply starts to outstrip demand. Yields are at their lowest (values at their highest). Speculative development is common. “This time is different” mentality prevails.
4. Slump / Recession Falling rents, rising vacancy, no new development, distressed sales Rents fall. Yields rise (values fall). Tenants fail or hand back space. Development stops. Banks restrict lending. Forced sellers create distressed opportunities.

Historical UK Property Cycles

  • Late 1980s boom / early 1990s crash: Speculative office development in London flooded the market. The recession of 1990–92 caused capital values to fall by 25%+ and several property companies went bust.
  • 2003–2007 boom: Low interest rates, easy credit, and a strong economy drove values to record highs. Commercial property total returns exceeded 15% per annum in some years.
  • 2007–2009 Global Financial Crisis: The most severe downturn since the 1930s. UK commercial property values fell by approximately 44% from peak to trough. Transaction volumes collapsed. Debt markets froze.
  • 2009–2019 recovery: A prolonged recovery, initially led by Central London offices, then spreading to other sectors and regions. Logistics/industrial became the best-performing sector.
  • 2020 COVID shock: A sudden, sector-specific shock. Retail and hospitality were devastated. Offices saw a demand reset. Logistics boomed. Not a traditional cycle — more of an accelerant for existing trends.
  • 2022–2024 rate rise correction: The Bank of England raised interest rates from 0.1% to 5.25%. This caused yields to rise (values to fall) across all sectors, but particularly in those that had been most aggressively priced.

In property, the cycle is your friend if you understand it and your enemy if you ignore it. The worst investments are made at the top of the market by people who are convinced the good times will last forever.

Key Terminology

Day 1

The property industry has its own language. You will hear these terms every day. Learn them now and they will become second nature.

Yield
The annual rent expressed as a percentage of the capital value. A £1m property producing £60,000 pa rent has a yield of 6%.
Freehold
Outright ownership of land and buildings with no time limit. The most complete form of ownership in English law.
Leasehold
The right to occupy property for a fixed term under a lease. The tenant (lessee) pays rent to the freeholder (lessor).
Covenant Strength
The financial reliability of a tenant. A “strong covenant” (e.g., government, major PLC) reduces void risk and increases value.
Passing Rent
The actual rent currently being paid under the lease. May differ from the market rent (ERV).
ERV (Estimated Rental Value)
The surveyor’s estimate of the market rent achievable for a property today. Used in valuations and rent reviews.
FRI Lease
Full Repairing and Insuring lease. The tenant is responsible for all repairs and insuring the building. Standard in commercial property.
Void
A vacant period when the property is unoccupied and producing no rental income. Also called “vacancy”.
Rent Review
A contractual mechanism to adjust the rent during the lease term, usually every 5 years, typically to the higher of the passing rent or market rent (upward only).
Break Clause
A provision in a lease allowing the tenant (or sometimes the landlord) to terminate the lease early on a specified date, subject to conditions.
Dilapidations
The cost of repairing a property to the condition required by the lease at the end of the term. A surveyor prepares a “schedule of dilapidations”.
Service Charge
A charge levied on tenants to recover the cost of maintaining and managing common areas, typically in multi-let buildings.
WAULT
Weighted Average Unexpired Lease Term. A measure of the average remaining lease length across a portfolio, weighted by rental income.
Rack-Rented
A property where the passing rent equals the ERV. The property is “at market rent”.
Reversionary
A property where the passing rent is below the ERV. The rent is expected to increase (revert) at the next rent review or lease renewal.
Over-Rented
A property where the passing rent exceeds the ERV. This can happen when market rents fall after the last rent review.
Prime
The best quality property in the best locations with the strongest tenants. Prime commands the lowest yields and highest rents.
Secondary
Property that is not prime. Could be lower quality, weaker location, or shorter lease. Higher risk = higher yield.
Cap Rate
Capitalisation rate — effectively the same as yield. More commonly used in the US market but increasingly seen in the UK.
Heads of Terms
A summary of the key commercial terms of a deal (rent, term, breaks, incentives) agreed “subject to contract” before lawyers prepare the lease.
SDLT
Stamp Duty Land Tax — a tax paid by the buyer on property purchases. Commercial rates differ from residential rates.
EPC (Energy Performance Certificate)
A rating from A (most efficient) to G (least efficient). Since April 2023, it is unlawful to let a commercial property with an EPC rating below E.

Yield is the relationship between annual rent and capital value. A £2 million warehouse at 5% yield produces £100,000 per annum in rent. Lower yields generally indicate lower-risk (prime) assets, while higher yields indicate higher-risk (secondary) assets.

Freehold vs Leasehold

Day 2

Two Forms of Property Ownership

In England and Wales, there are two fundamental types of property interest: freehold and leasehold. Understanding the difference is essential — it affects ownership rights, value, and the way property is managed and transacted.

Freehold (Fee Simple)

The freehold is the most complete form of property ownership. The freeholder owns the land and everything on it permanently — there is no time limit. When you buy a freehold, you own it until you sell it, give it away, or die (at which point it passes to your heirs or beneficiaries).

  • The freeholder has the right to use, develop (subject to planning), lease, mortgage, or sell the property
  • A freeholder who lets the property to a tenant becomes the landlord (or lessor)
  • Most commercial investment property is held on a freehold basis
  • The freehold is registered at the Land Registry with a unique title number

Leasehold

A leasehold is the right to occupy and use property for a fixed period, granted by the freeholder (or a superior leaseholder) under a lease. The leaseholder (tenant) pays rent and must comply with the terms of the lease.

  • A lease is a contract — it defines the rights and obligations of both landlord and tenant
  • The lease has a term (e.g., 10 years, 15 years, 25 years) after which the right to occupy expires
  • A leasehold interest is a wasting asset — it diminishes in value as the term shortens
  • Leasehold interests can themselves be sold (assigned) or sublet, subject to the terms of the lease
  • The value of a long leasehold (e.g., 999 years) is very close to the freehold value

Key Concept: The Profit Rent

If a tenant holds a lease at £50,000 pa but the market rent (ERV) is £70,000 pa, the tenant enjoys a profit rent of £20,000 pa. This profit rent has a capital value and can make the leasehold interest valuable enough to sell. Conversely, if the passing rent exceeds the ERV (the tenant is over-rented), the leasehold interest may have a negative value.

Anatomy of a Commercial Lease

Day 2

What is a Commercial Lease?

A commercial lease is a legally binding contract between a landlord and a tenant that grants the tenant the right to occupy and use property for business purposes, in return for rent. A typical commercial lease is 20–50 pages long and is prepared by solicitors, but the commercial terms are negotiated by surveyors.

Key Clauses in a Commercial Lease

ClauseWhat It Does
DemiseDefines exactly what is being let — the physical extent of the property, often by reference to a plan
TermThe length of the lease (e.g., “10 years from 1 January 2026”). The term is the most fundamental element.
RentThe initial annual rent, when it is payable (usually quarterly in advance on the English quarter days: 25 March, 24 June, 29 September, 25 December).
Rent ReviewThe mechanism for adjusting rent during the term. Typically upward-only to open market rental value every 5 years.
Break ClauseAn option for the tenant (or landlord) to end the lease early on specified dates, subject to conditions (e.g., giving 6 months’ notice, being up to date with rent, giving vacant possession).
User ClauseRestricts what the property can be used for (e.g., “B1 office use only”). Narrow user clauses limit flexibility and can reduce the rental value at review.
Repair CovenantWho is responsible for repairs. In a single-let building, this is usually a full repairing obligation on the tenant (FRI). In a multi-let building, the landlord typically repairs and recovers costs via the service charge.
InsuranceWho insures the building. Usually the landlord insures and recovers the premium from the tenant.
AlienationWhether the tenant can assign (sell) the lease to someone else, or sublet part of the property. Usually permitted with the landlord’s consent (not to be unreasonably withheld).
AlterationsWhether the tenant can alter the property. Usually: no structural alterations, non-structural alterations with landlord’s consent.
Service ChargeIn multi-let buildings, the tenant’s contribution to the cost of maintaining common areas, insurance, and management.
Yield-Up / DilapidationsThe condition in which the tenant must return the property at lease end. This often leads to a financial claim (dilapidations) if the property is not in the required condition.

The lease is the DNA of a property investment. Every clause affects value. A surveyor who cannot read and understand a lease is like a doctor who cannot read an X-ray.

What are Heads of Terms?

Heads of Terms (HoTs) are a summary of the key commercial terms agreed between landlord and tenant before solicitors begin drafting the lease. They are agreed “subject to contract”, meaning they are not legally binding (with limited exceptions). The surveyor is typically responsible for negotiating and documenting the HoTs.

The Letting Process (Step by Step)

  1. Marketing: The landlord’s agent markets the property (online portals, boards, mailshots, brochures)
  2. Viewings: Prospective tenants are shown the property
  3. Offers: Interested tenants submit offers, usually via their agent
  4. Heads of Terms: The landlord’s agent and the tenant’s agent negotiate and agree HoTs
  5. Referencing: The landlord takes references on the tenant (accounts, bank reference, trade references)
  6. Solicitors instructed: Both sides instruct solicitors. The landlord’s solicitor drafts the lease.
  7. Lease negotiation: Solicitors negotiate the detailed lease terms. Surveyors may need to resolve commercial points.
  8. Completion: The lease is signed (or executed as a deed for terms over 7 years), the tenant pays any deposit and the first quarter’s rent, and the tenant takes occupation.

The Landlord and Tenant Act 1954

Day 2

Security of Tenure

The Landlord and Tenant Act 1954 (Part II) is one of the most important pieces of legislation in commercial property. It gives business tenants security of tenure — the right to remain in occupation and renew their lease when it expires, even if the landlord does not want to renew.

How It Works

  • At the end of the contractual lease term, the tenancy does not simply end. It “holds over” on the same terms until either party takes action.
  • The tenant can serve a request for a new tenancy (Section 26 notice)
  • The landlord can serve a notice to terminate (Section 25 notice), either offering a new lease on proposed terms, or opposing renewal
  • If the landlord opposes renewal, they must prove one of the statutory grounds in Section 30(1) of the Act

Grounds for Opposing Renewal (Section 30)

GroundDescriptionCompensation?
(a)Tenant’s failure to repairNo
(b)Persistent delay in paying rentNo
(c)Other substantial breaches of the leaseNo
(d)Landlord has offered suitable alternative accommodationNo
(e)Tenancy created by sub-letting of part, and higher rent obtainable on letting as a wholeNo
(f)Landlord intends to demolish or reconstructYes
(g)Landlord intends to occupy the property themselvesYes

Grounds (f) and (g) are the most commonly used. They require the landlord to prove a genuine intention, not just a vague wish.

Contracting Out of the 1954 Act

Landlords and tenants can agree to exclude the security of tenure provisions before the lease is granted. This is called “contracting out”. If a lease is contracted out, the tenant has no right to renew at expiry.

The process requires the landlord to serve a warning notice on the tenant, and the tenant must sign a declaration (or statutory declaration) confirming they understand the consequences. Many modern short leases (e.g., 3–5 years) are contracted out.

Lease Events

Day 2

What are Lease Events?

Lease events are key dates during the term of a lease that trigger action by either the landlord, the tenant, or both. Missing a lease event can have serious financial consequences. Managing lease events is a core skill for property surveyors and managers.

Rent Reviews

Most commercial leases contain rent review provisions allowing the rent to be adjusted periodically, typically every 5 years.

  • Upward-only open market rent review: The rent is reviewed to the higher of the current passing rent or the open market rental value. This is the standard form in the UK.
  • RPI-linked review: The rent is adjusted in line with the Retail Prices Index. Less common but found in some longer leases, particularly in the alternatives sector.
  • Fixed increase: The rent increases by a predetermined amount or percentage (e.g., +10% every 5 years). Provides certainty but no relationship to market conditions.
  • The process involves the landlord serving a trigger notice, the parties then negotiate, and if they cannot agree, the matter can be referred to an independent surveyor as arbitrator or independent expert.

Break Clauses

A break clause gives one or both parties the option to terminate the lease early. Break clauses are extremely important and the conditions must be strictly observed.

  • Notice: The party exercising the break must give written notice, typically 6–12 months before the break date
  • Conditions: Common conditions include that the tenant must be up to date with rent, must give vacant possession, and must have complied with all lease obligations
  • Strict compliance: Courts have consistently held that break conditions must be precisely met. Even a small breach (e.g., a few items of furniture left behind, preventing “vacant possession”) can invalidate the break

Lease Expiry

At the end of the contractual term:

  • If the lease is inside the 1954 Act: the tenancy continues automatically on the same terms until formally terminated or renewed
  • If the lease is contracted out: the tenant must vacate on the expiry date. There is no right to renew.
  • In both cases, the landlord may have a dilapidations claim if the property is not in the condition required by the lease

Time is of the Essence

Many lease events are time-sensitive. Missing a deadline can mean:

  • A rent review deemed to have been agreed at the landlord’s proposed figure
  • A break clause that cannot be exercised, locking the tenant in for the remainder of the term
  • A renewal deadline that passes, weakening the tenant’s negotiating position

This is why lease event management (diary systems, reminder processes) is critical in property management.

Assignment, Subletting & Alienation

Day 2

What is Alienation?

Alienation is the legal term for a tenant transferring their interest in a lease to a third party. It covers both assignment (transferring the whole lease) and subletting (granting a sub-lease of part or all of the property).

Assignment

An assignment is the transfer of the tenant’s entire leasehold interest to a new tenant. The outgoing tenant drops out and the incoming tenant (assignee) takes on all the obligations under the lease.

  • Most leases permit assignment of the whole with the landlord’s prior written consent, not to be unreasonably withheld or delayed
  • The landlord will want to check the financial strength (covenant) of the proposed assignee
  • The landlord may require an Authorised Guarantee Agreement (AGA) from the outgoing tenant, guaranteeing the assignee’s obligations
  • Assignment of part is almost always prohibited

Subletting

Subletting is where the tenant grants a new lease (sub-lease) to a sub-tenant for part or all of the property. The original tenant remains bound by the head lease.

  • The sub-lease must be on terms consistent with (and no less onerous than) the head lease
  • The sub-rent should be at or above market rent — landlords will not want the tenant creating artificially low sub-rents that could depress the rental value at review
  • The sub-tenant has their own relationship with the tenant (as sub-landlord), not with the freeholder
  • If the head lease ends, the sub-lease typically falls away too

Why Alienation Matters

Alienation provisions affect the liquidity of a leasehold interest. A lease that can be freely assigned is more valuable than one with tight restrictions, because the tenant has a way out if their business needs change. From the landlord’s perspective, restrictions protect them from ending up with a financially weak tenant.

8 Questions
Question 1 of 8
What is the fundamental difference between freehold and leasehold ownership?
A freehold is perpetual ownership — it lasts forever. A leasehold is the right to occupy for a fixed period. The leasehold is a wasting asset because its value diminishes as the remaining term shortens. The freeholder owns the underlying interest and receives rent from the leaseholder.
Question 2 of 8
How is commercial rent in the UK typically paid?
Commercial rent in the UK is traditionally paid quarterly in advance on the English quarter days: 25 March (Lady Day), 24 June (Midsummer Day), 29 September (Michaelmas Day), and 25 December (Christmas Day). Some modern leases are moving to monthly payment, but quarterly remains the standard.
Question 3 of 8
What does the Landlord and Tenant Act 1954 (Part II) provide for business tenants?
The 1954 Act gives qualifying business tenants security of tenure: the right to remain in occupation when the lease expires and to apply for a new tenancy. The landlord can only oppose renewal on specific statutory grounds (e.g., redevelopment, own occupation). It does not give tenants the right to buy the freehold or prevent rent increases.
Question 4 of 8
What happens when a lease is “contracted out” of the Landlord and Tenant Act 1954?
When a lease is contracted out of the 1954 Act, the tenant waives their security of tenure. At the end of the lease term, the tenant must vacate. There is no right to remain, no right to renew, and no right to compensation. The process requires a formal warning notice from the landlord and a declaration from the tenant.
Question 5 of 8
What is the most common rent review pattern in UK commercial leases?
The standard rent review pattern in UK commercial leases is a 5-yearly upward-only review to open market rental value. “Upward only” means the rent can only stay the same or increase; it can never be reduced at review, even if market rents have fallen.
Question 6 of 8
A tenant wants to exercise a break clause. Their lease says they must give 6 months’ notice and provide vacant possession. They give notice but leave some furniture behind. What happens?
Break clause conditions require strict compliance. “Vacant possession” means the property must be completely empty and free from people and chattels. Leaving furniture behind can prevent vacant possession and cause the break to fail, meaning the tenant remains liable for rent for the remainder of the lease term. This has been confirmed by case law.
Question 7 of 8
What is an Authorised Guarantee Agreement (AGA)?
An AGA is a guarantee given by the outgoing tenant when assigning a lease, guaranteeing that the incoming assignee will comply with the lease obligations. If the assignee defaults on rent, the outgoing tenant (guarantor under the AGA) becomes liable. AGAs last until the next assignment and provide the landlord with additional security.
Question 8 of 8
What is the difference between assignment and subletting?
In an assignment, the entire leasehold interest is transferred to the assignee, and the outgoing tenant drops out (though they may give an AGA). In subletting, the original tenant remains bound by the head lease and creates a new, shorter sub-lease with a sub-tenant. The sub-tenant’s relationship is with the tenant, not with the freeholder.

The Five Methods of Valuation

Day 3

Why Valuation Matters

Valuation is at the heart of everything in property. Every transaction, every investment decision, every loan, and every financial account depends on knowing what a property is worth. As a surveyor, you will be expected to understand valuation principles from day one.

There are five recognised methods of valuation, and the choice of method depends on the type of property and the purpose of the valuation.

1. The Comparison Method

The most widely used method. Value is determined by comparing the subject property to similar properties that have recently been let or sold.

  • Used for: most standard commercial property — offices, shops, industrial units
  • Requires: good comparable evidence (recent transactions of similar properties in similar locations)
  • The surveyor adjusts comparables for differences in size, quality, location, lease terms, and date
  • Example: an office in Sheffield city centre was recently let at £22 per sq ft. A similar office nearby should let at a similar rate, adjusted for any differences.

2. The Investment Method

Used to value property that produces (or will produce) an income stream. This is the dominant method for investment property.

  • The rental income is capitalised (multiplied by a factor derived from the yield) to give a capital value
  • Simple formula: Capital Value = Rent ÷ Yield (or Rent × Years Purchase)
  • Example: A shop producing £50,000 pa rent, valued at a 5% yield = £50,000 ÷ 0.05 = £1,000,000
  • For reversionary properties (where the passing rent differs from the ERV), the valuation uses a term and reversion or layer approach
  • The yield used reflects the risk profile: stronger covenant, longer lease, better location = lower yield = higher value

3. The Profits Method (Accounts Method)

Used for “operational” properties where the value is tied to the business trading from them, and where there is limited comparable evidence.

  • Used for: hotels, pubs, petrol stations, care homes, cinemas, leisure
  • The valuer analyses the accounts of the business to estimate the profit that a “reasonably efficient operator” would achieve
  • A portion of that profit is attributed to the property as rent, then capitalised

4. The Contractor’s Method (Cost Method)

A method of last resort, used when no market evidence exists and the property does not produce income.

  • Used for: specialist properties — schools, churches, prisons, town halls, military bases
  • Estimates the cost of replacing the building (adjusted for age and depreciation) plus the value of the land
  • Mainly used for rating (business rates) and insurance valuations

5. The Residual Method

Used to value development sites by working backwards from the completed development value.

  • Used for: land with development potential
  • Formula: Land Value = Gross Development Value (GDV) − Construction Costs − Professional Fees − Finance Costs − Developer’s Profit
  • Example: a site that will produce a completed development worth £10m, with total costs of £7m and developer’s profit of £1.5m, has a residual land value of £1.5m
  • Extremely sensitive to assumptions — small changes in GDV or costs produce large swings in land value

Yields Explained in Depth

Day 3

What is a Yield?

A yield is the annual rental income expressed as a percentage of the capital value. It is the single most important number in property investment — it tells you the rate of return and, crucially, the level of risk the market perceives in a property.

Key principle: Lower yield = lower risk = higher price. Higher yield = higher risk = lower price.

Types of Yield

Yield TypeFormulaWhat It Tells You
Initial Yield Passing Rent ÷ Purchase Price The return on day one based on the current rent and purchase price. The most commonly quoted yield.
Reversionary Yield ERV ÷ Purchase Price The return if/when the rent is reviewed to market level. If the property is reversionary (under-rented), the reversionary yield will be higher than the initial yield.
Equivalent Yield IRR of the income profile A single blended yield that, when applied to the term income and the reversionary income, gives the same capital value. Used in professional valuations to account for the dual income stream.
Net Initial Yield Passing Rent ÷ (Purchase Price + Costs) Like initial yield but accounts for purchaser’s costs (SDLT, legal fees, agency fees — typically ~6.8% of the purchase price). The “true” yield to a buyer.
True Equivalent Yield Equivalent yield adjusted for quarterly-in-advance rent Adjusts the equivalent yield to reflect the fact that rent is received quarterly in advance, not annually in arrears.

What Determines the Yield?

The yield reflects the market’s assessment of risk. Factors that drive yield include:

  • Location: Prime London = lowest yields. Regional secondary = highest yields.
  • Tenant covenant: Government or FTSE 100 tenant = low yield. Unknown SME = higher yield.
  • Lease length: Long unexpired term = low yield (secure income). Short term = higher yield.
  • Building quality: New, Grade A, sustainable = low yield. Dated, poor condition = higher yield.
  • Sector: Logistics prime might be 4–5%; secondary retail might be 8–12%.
  • Interest rates: When Bank of England base rate rises, property yields tend to rise too (because other investments become more competitive).

Worked Example

You are valuing a warehouse in Sheffield:

  • Passing rent: £200,000 pa
  • ERV: £250,000 pa (the property is under-rented / reversionary)
  • Purchase price: £3,333,333

Initial yield: £200,000 ÷ £3,333,333 = 6.0%

Reversionary yield: £250,000 ÷ £3,333,333 = 7.5%

The initial yield of 6.0% is what the investor receives today. The reversionary yield of 7.5% is what they expect to receive once the rent is reviewed to market level. This “reversion” makes the property attractive — there is built-in rental growth.

Comparable Evidence

Day 3

The Foundation of Valuation

The comparison method relies on comparable evidence — data from recent transactions involving similar properties. The quality of your valuation depends entirely on the quality of your comparables. “Garbage in, garbage out” applies absolutely.

What Makes a Good Comparable?

  • Recent: The more recent the transaction, the more reliable it is. Ideally within the last 6–12 months.
  • Similar property: Same type (office, industrial, retail), similar size, quality, and specification.
  • Similar location: Same town, similar micro-location (e.g., city centre vs out-of-town).
  • Arm’s length: The transaction must be between willing parties acting independently. Sales between related companies or at auction (which may be forced) need careful interpretation.
  • Verifiable: You need to know the actual terms — headline rent, incentives, lease term. Market rumour is not evidence.

Where to Find Comparable Evidence

  • CoStar / EGi: The main commercial property databases in the UK. Most firms subscribe to one or both.
  • Land Registry: Records all property sales in England and Wales (free to access online for basic information).
  • In-house records: Your own firm’s transaction data. Particularly valuable because you know the full terms.
  • Agent contacts: Speaking to other agents in the market. The property world is relationship-driven.
  • RICS / BCIS: The RICS maintains various data sources, and BCIS provides construction cost data.

Adjusting Comparables

No two properties are identical, so comparables must be adjusted to reflect differences. Common adjustments include:

  • Incentives: Headline rent of £25 psf with 12 months rent-free on a 10-year lease. The effective (net effective) rent is lower. You must “devalue” the incentive to find the true rent.
  • Size: Smaller units often achieve higher rents per sq ft than larger units (the “quantum adjustment”).
  • Floor level: Upper floors in offices may command a premium for views. Ground floors in retail command the highest rents.
  • Condition: A newly refurbished building vs one that needs work.
  • Date: If the market has moved since the comparable transaction, an adjustment for time may be needed.

The RICS Red Book

Day 3

What is the Red Book?

The RICS Valuation – Global Standards, universally known as the “Red Book”, is the definitive set of rules and standards that govern property valuations carried out by RICS members. Any formal valuation must comply with the Red Book.

Key Concepts

  • Market Value: The estimated amount for which an asset should exchange on the valuation date between a willing buyer and a willing seller in an arm’s-length transaction after proper marketing and where the parties had each acted knowledgeably, prudently, and without compulsion.
  • Market Rent: The estimated amount for which a property should be leased on the valuation date between a willing lessor and a willing lessee on appropriate lease terms.
  • Fair Value: Used for financial reporting (IFRS 13). The price that would be received to sell an asset in an orderly transaction between market participants.

Why Does the Red Book Matter?

  • It ensures consistency — valuations follow the same approach regardless of which surveyor prepares them
  • It protects the public interest — valuations relied upon by banks, investors, and pension funds must be prepared to a professional standard
  • It provides liability protection — if a valuer follows the Red Book, they are better protected against negligence claims
  • It is required for secured lending (bank valuations), company accounts, pension fund valuations, and public sector work

Red Book Valuation Requirements

  • The valuer must be appropriately qualified and experienced
  • The valuer must be independent — no conflict of interest
  • The report must include: basis of value, valuation date, purpose, special assumptions, inspection details, and the valuation figure
  • The valuer must carry Professional Indemnity Insurance (PII)
  • The valuation must be based on evidence, not opinion alone

Investment Analysis: IRR, NPV & DCF

Day 3

Beyond Simple Yields

While yields are the day-to-day language of property investment, sophisticated investors use more advanced techniques to analyse whether a property is a good investment. The three key concepts are IRR, NPV, and DCF.

Discounted Cash Flow (DCF)

A DCF models all future cash flows from a property (rent received, costs paid, and eventual sale proceeds) and discounts them back to their present value using a discount rate.

  • The principle is that £1 received today is worth more than £1 received in 10 years (because of inflation, risk, and opportunity cost)
  • The discount rate reflects the investor’s required rate of return, typically 7–12% for property
  • A DCF explicitly models rental growth, void periods, refurbishment costs, and exit values

Net Present Value (NPV)

NPV is the sum of all discounted future cash flows minus the initial investment. It tells you whether an investment creates or destroys value.

  • NPV > 0: The investment exceeds the required return — it creates value. Buy.
  • NPV = 0: The investment exactly meets the required return. Break even.
  • NPV < 0: The investment falls short of the required return — it destroys value. Do not buy.

Internal Rate of Return (IRR)

The IRR is the discount rate at which the NPV equals zero. In simple terms, it is the annualised total return the investor will achieve if all assumptions in the cash flow prove correct.

  • If the IRR exceeds the investor’s target return (hurdle rate), the investment is attractive
  • IRR allows comparison between different investments with different profiles
  • Property IRRs for core investments are typically 6–10%; for value-add or opportunistic strategies, 12–20%+

A Health Warning

DCF models are only as good as their assumptions. Small changes in rental growth, exit yield, or void assumptions can dramatically change the output. A DCF gives you a precise answer, but that precision can be misleading if the inputs are wrong. Always question the assumptions.

How Retail Rent is Calculated: Zoning

High street shop rents in the UK have traditionally been calculated using the zoning method:

  • The shop is divided into zones from the front (zone A, nearest the shop front) to the back
  • Each zone is typically 20 feet deep (6.1 metres)
  • Zone A is the most valuable (100% of Zone A rate). Zone B is 50%. Zone C is 25%. The remainder is 12.5% (or an overall rate)
  • All rents are expressed in terms of Zone A equivalent (ITZA) for comparison purposes
  • Example: if Zone A rent is £100 per sq ft ITZA, a shop with 500 sq ft on Zone A and 500 sq ft on Zone B has an ITZA of 500 + (500 × 0.5) = 750 sq ft ITZA. Rent = 750 × £100 = £75,000 pa

The UK is one of the most advanced e-commerce markets in the world. Online penetration of approximately 27% of total retail sales has fundamentally changed the property landscape:

  • High street vacancy rates have risen, particularly in secondary locations
  • Retailers are operating fewer, larger flagship stores supplemented by online fulfilment
  • Experiential retail (restaurants, gyms, cinemas, health clinics) is replacing traditional shops
  • Some retail space is being converted to alternative uses (residential, offices, healthcare)
  • Town centre planning policy is evolving to support diversification

What is a Commercial Property Agent?

Incentives & Effective Rent

Day 4

What are Tenant Incentives?

In most commercial lettings, the landlord offers incentives to attract tenants. These reduce the effective cost of occupation and are a normal part of the market. As a surveyor, you must understand how to quantify them and their impact on rental value.

Common Incentive Types

IncentiveDescription
Rent-Free PeriodA period at the start of the lease during which the tenant pays no rent. Typically 1–24 months depending on the lease term and market conditions. On a 10-year lease, 6–12 months rent-free is common.
Capital ContributionA cash payment from the landlord towards the tenant’s fit-out costs. An alternative to rent-free. Typically £10–40 per sq ft.
Stepped RentRent starts low and increases in stages over the first few years (e.g., £15 psf year 1, £18 psf year 2, £22 psf year 3+).
Break ClauseA tenant break clause is itself an incentive — it reduces the tenant’s commitment risk.

Headline Rent vs Effective Rent

This distinction is critical:

  • Headline rent: The “face value” rent stated in the lease. What is written down. What appears in the Heads of Terms.
  • Effective rent (net effective rent): The actual cost to the tenant after accounting for incentives, spread over the lease term (or to the first break).

Example: 5,000 sq ft office. Headline rent £22 psf = £110,000 pa. 10-year lease. 12 months rent-free.

Total rent payable over 10 years: £110,000 × 9 = £990,000

Effective rent per annum: £990,000 ÷ 10 = £99,000 pa (or £19.80 psf effective)

Understanding the difference between headline and effective rent is essential when analysing comparable evidence and advising clients.

Business Rates & the VOA

Day 4

What are Business Rates?

Business rates (formally National Non-Domestic Rates, or NNDR) are a tax levied on most commercial properties. They are the commercial equivalent of council tax and represent a significant occupational cost for businesses.

How Business Rates are Calculated

  • Every commercial property has a Rateable Value (RV) set by the Valuation Office Agency (VOA)
  • The RV is based on the estimated annual rental value of the property at a specific valuation date (the “Antecedent Valuation Date” or AVD)
  • The annual rates bill = RV × Uniform Business Rate (UBR) multiplier (set annually by government, currently approximately 50–55p in the pound)
  • Example: RV of £100,000 × UBR of 0.512 = annual rates bill of £51,200
  • The VOA conducts a revaluation periodically (currently every 3 years from 2023) to update RVs

The Valuation Office Agency (VOA)

The VOA is an executive agency of HM Revenue and Customs. It is responsible for:

  • Setting the rateable value of every non-domestic property in England and Wales
  • Maintaining the rating list
  • Dealing with appeals (via the Check, Challenge, Appeal process)
  • The VOA uses the rental comparison method for most properties and the contractor’s method for specialist properties

Empty Rates

Landlords must pay business rates on empty commercial property (after an initial exemption period). This is a significant cost during void periods:

  • Offices and retail: 3 months empty rate exemption, then full rates payable
  • Industrial / warehouses: 6 months empty rate exemption, then full rates payable
  • This creates a strong financial incentive for landlords to keep properties occupied
  • Some landlords use strategies to mitigate empty rates (short-term letting, charitable occupation, demolition)

Clear height is the usable vertical space inside a warehouse, measured from the finished floor to the underside of the structural roof element (typically the haunch of a portal frame). Modern big box logistics warehouses have clear heights of 12–15 metres, allowing high-bay racking and efficient use of the building’s cube.
Question 5 of 8
What is a typical letting fee for a landlord’s agent on a commercial letting?
A landlord’s agent typically charges 10–15% of the first year’s rent for a letting instruction. This can vary based on the market, the value of the deal, and whether joint agents are involved. For example, on a letting at £100,000 pa, the agent’s fee would be £10,000–15,000 plus VAT.
Question 6 of 8
An office is let at a headline rent of £100,000 pa on a 10-year lease with 12 months rent-free. What is the effective rent per annum?
The tenant pays rent for 9 out of 10 years: £100,000 × 9 = £900,000 total. Spread over the 10-year term: £900,000 ÷ 10 = £90,000 pa effective. The headline rent is £100,000 but the effective rent is only £90,000 once the incentive is taken into account.
Question 7 of 8
Who sets the rateable value of commercial properties for business rates purposes?
The Valuation Office Agency (VOA), an executive agency of HMRC, is responsible for assessing and maintaining the rateable values of all non-domestic property in England and Wales. The local council collects the rates, and the government sets the multiplier, but it is the VOA that determines the rateable value.
Question 8 of 8
How long is the business rates exemption for an empty industrial property before the landlord must pay empty rates?
Empty industrial and warehouse properties receive a 6-month exemption from business rates. After 6 months, the landlord must pay full empty rates. For offices and retail, the exemption is only 3 months. This difference reflects the longer typical void periods in the industrial sector.

RICS in Depth

Day 5

What is RICS?

The Royal Institution of Chartered Surveyors (RICS) is the global professional body for surveyors, established in 1868 and granted a Royal Charter in 1881. RICS sets and enforces the standards that govern the work of its 130,000+ members in 146 countries worldwide.

The letters MRICS (Member) or FRICS (Fellow) after a surveyor’s name signify that they have met rigorous standards of education, training, and professional conduct. It is the gold standard in the property profession.

What RICS Does

  • Sets professional standards: Including the Red Book (valuation), the Service Charge Code, professional statements, and guidance notes
  • Regulates members: RICS can investigate complaints, discipline members, and ultimately expel them from the profession
  • Accredits qualifications: RICS accredits university courses (like your CEM degree) and oversees the APC assessment
  • Promotes standards globally: RICS standards are adopted in many countries, making it easier for UK surveyors to work internationally
  • Protects the public: By ensuring surveyors are properly qualified, insured, and ethical
  • Provides market data: RICS publishes market surveys, economic research, and industry reports

RICS Regulation

RICS-regulated firms must comply with strict rules including:

  • Maintaining Professional Indemnity Insurance (PII)
  • Handling client money in accordance with RICS Client Money Rules
  • Operating a complaints handling procedure
  • Complying with Anti-Money Laundering (AML) regulations
  • Continuing Professional Development (CPD) for all members: minimum 20 hours per year
  • Regular firm inspections and audits

The APC Pathway

What is the APC?

The Assessment of Professional Competence (APC) is the final assessment you must pass to become a Chartered Surveyor (MRICS). It tests whether you have the practical skills, knowledge, and professional competence to practise independently.

The APC Structure

ElementDescription
PathwayYou choose an APC pathway that matches your specialism (e.g., Commercial Real Estate). Each pathway has mandatory and optional competencies.
CompetenciesTypically 8–10 competencies. Each must be demonstrated at Level 1 (knowledge), Level 2 (application), or Level 3 (reasoned advice & depth). You must achieve the specified level for each competency.
Experience RecordA structured diary recording your practical experience against each competency. Updated regularly (ideally weekly).
Case StudyA 3,000-word case study of a real project you have worked on, demonstrating your key competencies in action.
Summary of ExperienceA 2-page summary of your experience, used by the assessors to guide the interview.
Final Assessment InterviewA 1-hour interview with a panel of assessors. Typically: 10 minutes on the case study, 40 minutes of competency-based questions, 10 minutes on ethics and professional standards.

Competency Levels Explained

  • Level 1 — Knowledge: You can demonstrate that you know about the subject and understand its principles. You have studied it and can describe it.
  • Level 2 — Application: You have applied your knowledge in practice. You have done it, been involved in it, and can explain your role and what you did.
  • Level 3 — Reasoned Advice: You can provide well-reasoned advice to clients and others. You can justify your approach, consider alternatives, and demonstrate professional judgement.

The APC is not about memorising theory. It is about demonstrating that you can do the job competently and ethically in the real world.

Timeline

As an apprentice surveyor, your timeline to MRICS will be approximately:

  • Year 1–2: University study (CEM) + workplace experience. Begin logging competencies.
  • Year 3–4: Continue study + deeper workplace experience. Build competencies to required levels.
  • Year 4–5: Final year of degree. Prepare case study and summary. Submit APC application.
  • APC Interview: Typically taken within 6–12 months of completing your degree and experience requirements.

RICS Ethical Standards

Day 5

The Five Ethical Principles

RICS members must adhere to five ethical principles. These are tested in the APC interview and are the foundation of professional conduct throughout your career. You must know them cold.

1. Act with Integrity

Be honest and straightforward in all that you do. Never mislead clients, colleagues, or the public. If you make a mistake, own it immediately.

Scenario: You discover that a property you are marketing has a hidden defect that significantly affects value. What do you do? You must disclose it. Concealing a material fact would be a serious breach of integrity.

2. Always Provide a High Standard of Service

Be diligent, competent, and act in a timely manner. Ensure your work meets the highest standards the client would reasonably expect. If a task is outside your competence, either develop the competence, get help, or refer it to someone qualified.

Scenario: You are asked to provide a valuation of a type of property you have never valued before. You should either work under the supervision of an experienced colleague or decline the instruction and refer the client to someone with the necessary expertise.

3. Act in a Way that Promotes Trust in the Profession

Behave in a way that upholds the reputation of the profession. Avoid actions that could bring RICS or the surveying profession into disrepute, both in your professional and personal conduct.

Scenario: A colleague suggests inflating a valuation figure to secure a lending deal. You must refuse, even if it means losing the instruction. Complicit overvaluation has destroyed careers and firms.

4. Treat Others with Respect

Treat everyone — clients, colleagues, competitors, and the public — with respect, courtesy, and consideration. Embrace diversity and never discriminate.

Scenario: During a rent review negotiation, the opposing surveyor takes a position you strongly disagree with. You debate the evidence professionally; you do not resort to personal attacks or bullying tactics.

5. Take Responsibility

Take responsibility for your actions. If something goes wrong, address it promptly. Consider the impact of your advice on others and the wider community. Raise concerns if you see unethical behaviour by others.

Scenario: You realise you gave a client incorrect advice about a lease event deadline. You immediately contact the client, explain the error, and take steps to mitigate any damage. You do not cover it up or hope nobody notices.

PII & Client Money Protection

Day 5

Professional Indemnity Insurance (PII)

PII is insurance that protects surveyors (and their clients) against claims arising from professional negligence. All RICS-regulated firms must carry PII. It is a mandatory condition of RICS regulation.

Why PII Matters

  • A surveyor’s advice can have financial consequences running into millions of pounds (e.g., a negligent valuation that leads a bank to over-lend)
  • PII covers the cost of defending a negligence claim and paying damages if the claim succeeds
  • Without PII, a single negligence claim could bankrupt a surveying firm
  • RICS sets minimum PII requirements based on the firm’s turnover and activities
  • Valuations for secured lending typically require very high levels of cover

Client Money

Client money is money held by a surveying firm on behalf of a client — for example, rent collected by a managing agent, deposits, or service charge funds. RICS has strict rules about client money handling:

  • Client money must be held in a separate, designated client bank account — never mixed with the firm’s own money
  • Proper accounting records must be maintained
  • Regular reconciliations must be performed
  • The firm must have a Client Money Protection Scheme
  • Annual audits of client money accounts are required
  • Mishandling client money is one of the most serious regulatory offences

A client asks you to conceal a known defect when marketing their property for sale. Which RICS ethical principle does this breach?

Question 3 of 10
A commercial tenant’s 10-year lease has expired, but the lease is inside the 1954 Act. What is the tenant’s position?
Under the Landlord and Tenant Act 1954, the tenancy continues after the contractual term expires (it “holds over”) on the same terms. The tenant can serve a Section 26 notice requesting a new tenancy. The landlord can only terminate by proving one of the statutory grounds in Section 30 of the Act.
Question 4 of 10
If yields compress from 6% to 5% on a property producing £120,000 pa, what happens to the capital value?
At 6% yield: £120,000 ÷ 0.06 = £2,000,000. At 5% yield: £120,000 ÷ 0.05 = £2,400,000. The value increases by £400,000 (20%). This is why “yield compression” is good for owners and “yield expansion” is bad. Yields and values move in opposite directions.
Question 5 of 10
A shop has 500 sq ft on Zone A (at £80 per sq ft ITZA) and 500 sq ft on Zone B. What is the total annual rent?
Zone A: 500 sq ft × £80 = £40,000. Zone B is valued at 50% of Zone A: 500 sq ft × £40 = £20,000. Total ITZA = 500 + 250 = 750 sq ft ITZA. Total rent: £40,000 + £20,000 = £60,000 pa. This demonstrates the zoning principle where each successive zone is worth half the previous one.

Why must all RICS-regulated firms carry Professional Indemnity Insurance (PII)?
PII protects clients against financial losses arising from a surveyor’s professional negligence. If a surveyor provides a negligent valuation that causes a bank to over-lend, or gives incorrect advice that leads to a client losing money, PII covers the cost of defending the claim and paying damages. It is mandatory for RICS-regulated firms.
