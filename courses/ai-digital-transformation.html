What is AI?

The Simple Definition

Artificial Intelligence (AI) is the ability of a computer to perform tasks that normally require human intelligence. That includes things like understanding language, recognising images, making decisions, and learning from experience.

You already use AI every day, even if you do not realise it. When your phone suggests the next word in a text message, that is AI. When Netflix recommends a show you might like, that is AI. When Google Maps finds the fastest route around traffic, that is AI.

Analogy: AI as a Very Fast Intern

Imagine hiring an intern who can read 10,000 documents in a minute, never forgets anything they have read, works 24 hours a day, and never complains. However, this intern has no common sense, cannot visit a building, and occasionally makes things up with complete confidence. That is roughly what AI is today: incredibly capable at processing information, but it still needs a knowledgeable human to check the work and make the final decisions.

A Brief History

  • 1950s: Alan Turing asks "Can machines think?" — the birth of the idea
  • 1960s-80s: Early rule-based systems. Computers could follow "if X then Y" instructions but could not truly learn
  • 1990s-2000s: Machine learning takes off. Instead of programming every rule, computers learn patterns from data
  • 2012: Deep learning breakthrough. Neural networks (inspired by the human brain) start beating humans at image recognition
  • 2017: Google publishes the "Transformer" paper — the architecture behind every modern AI chatbot
  • 2022: ChatGPT launches and AI enters the mainstream. The world changes overnight
  • 2023-2026: AI becomes a practical tool in every industry, including property. Hillway is at the forefront

Narrow AI vs General AI

There are two types of AI you will hear about:

Narrow AI (What We Have Now)

AI that is very good at one specific thing. A chess AI can beat any human at chess but cannot write an email. A language AI can write text but cannot drive a car. All AI tools you will use at Hillway are narrow AI, though the best ones are narrow in a very broad category: language.

General AI (Does Not Exist Yet)

AI that can do anything a human can do: understand context, learn new skills on the fly, feel emotions, have genuine creativity. This is what you see in science fiction films. It does not exist and most experts think it is still decades away, if it is even possible. Do not confuse today's AI with this.

Machine Learning: How AI Actually Learns

Traditional software follows rules that a programmer writes: "If the rent is over 50,000 pounds, flag it for review." Machine learning is different. Instead of writing rules, you give the computer thousands of examples and it figures out the patterns itself.

Analogy: Learning to Value Property

Think about how a surveyor learns to value commercial property. Nobody hands you a rulebook that says "a 2,000 sq ft office in Sheffield city centre is worth exactly X." Instead, you look at hundreds of comparable transactions, absorb the patterns, and develop an instinct. Machine learning works the same way: feed a computer enough data and it learns to spot the patterns that matter.

Large Language Models

What is a Large Language Model?

A Large Language Model (LLM) is a specific type of AI that has been trained on enormous amounts of text to understand and generate human language. When you chat with Claude or ChatGPT, you are talking to an LLM.

The word "large" refers to the size of the model (billions of mathematical parameters) and the amount of data it was trained on (essentially a significant portion of the internet, plus books, academic papers, code, and more).

How LLMs Work: The Transformer Architecture

Every modern LLM is built on the Transformer architecture, invented by Google in 2017. Here is the simplified version:

Analogy: The World's Best Autocomplete

You know how your phone predicts the next word when you are typing a message? An LLM does the same thing, but instead of predicting one word, it can predict thousands of words in sequence — and it has been trained on so much text that its predictions are remarkably intelligent. It does not "think" the way you do. It calculates the most likely next word, then the next, then the next. The result looks like genuine understanding because the patterns it has learned are extraordinarily sophisticated.

Key concepts:

  • Training: The model reads billions of pages of text and builds a mathematical model of how language works. This takes months and costs millions of pounds in computing power
  • Parameters: The "settings" inside the model that were adjusted during training. More parameters generally means more capable. Claude has hundreds of billions
  • Tokens: The units LLMs process. A token is roughly three-quarters of a word. "Surveyor" is two tokens. This matters because models have a limit on how many tokens they can process at once (their "context window")
  • Context window: How much text the model can "see" at once. Claude can handle 200,000 tokens — equivalent to reading a 500-page book in one go

What LLMs Can and Cannot Do

What LLMs Do Well

Writing and editing text. Summarising long documents. Answering questions about content you provide. Translating between languages. Writing code. Analysing data you paste in. Brainstorming ideas. Explaining complex concepts in simple terms.

What LLMs Cannot Do

Access the internet in real-time (unless given tools). Remember previous conversations (each chat starts fresh). Do maths reliably for complex calculations. Guarantee factual accuracy. Visit a property or physically inspect anything. Replace professional judgement.

Hallucinations: The Biggest Risk

A hallucination is when an AI generates information that sounds completely plausible but is factually wrong. For example, it might cite a court case that does not exist, or quote rental figures that it has made up.

Why This Matters for Surveyors

If you ask an LLM "What is the average office rent in Sheffield city centre?" it will give you a confident answer. But that number might be completely invented. As a surveyor working under RICS regulation, you cannot rely on AI-generated figures without verification. Always treat AI output as a first draft that needs professional checking — never as established fact.

Using Claude Chat

Getting Started at chat.anthropic.com

Claude's web interface at chat.anthropic.com is where you will do most of your day-to-day AI work. It works like a messaging conversation: you type a message (called a "prompt"), Claude responds, and you can continue the conversation back and forth.

Hillway has a team subscription so you will have access to all Claude models. Here is how to make the most of it:

Key Concepts

  • Prompt: The message you send to Claude. Think of it like a brief to a colleague — the clearer the brief, the better the output
  • Context: Everything Claude can "see" in your conversation. Claude does not know anything about you or your previous conversations unless you tell it in the current chat
  • Conversation: A single chat thread. Each new conversation starts completely fresh — Claude has no memory between conversations
  • Attachments: You can upload documents (PDFs, Word, Excel, images) for Claude to analyse. This is incredibly powerful for property work

Choosing the Right Format for Your Request

Claude can respond in different formats depending on what you need. The trick is to tell it:

  • Bullet points: "Summarise this lease in bullet points" — great for quick reference
  • Table format: "Create a comparison table of these three properties" — great for presenting options
  • Formal report: "Write a formal property report covering..." — great for client deliverables
  • Email draft: "Draft a professional email to the tenant about..." — great for correspondence
  • Step-by-step: "Walk me through the process for..." — great for learning

Top Tip: Start Every Chat with Context

Begin each conversation by telling Claude who you are and what you are working on. For example: "I am an apprentice surveyor at Hillway, a commercial property consultancy in Sheffield. I am working on [task]. Please keep your responses suitable for a professional property context."

Prompt Engineering

The Art of Asking Well

Prompt engineering is the skill of crafting your requests to AI so you get the best possible response. It is not about learning magic words — it is about being clear, specific, and structured. Think of it like writing a good brief for a colleague: the more context and specificity you provide, the better the output.

The 5 Principles of Good Prompts

1. Be Specific

Bad Prompt

"Tell me about commercial leases."

Good Prompt

"Explain the key differences between a full repairing and insuring (FRI) lease and an internal repairing lease for commercial property in England and Wales. I am an apprentice surveyor learning the basics."

2. Provide Context

Tell Claude what you are working on, who the audience is, and what you know already. "I am preparing a rent review memo for a partner. The property is a 5,000 sq ft office in Sheffield city centre. The lease is on an upward-only review to open market rent. The current passing rent is 65,000 pounds per annum."

3. Specify the Format

"Present this as a numbered list." "Format this as a professional email." "Create a table with columns for: property name, size, rent, and lease expiry." "Keep your response under 200 words."

4. Use Chain of Thought

For complex questions, ask Claude to think step by step: "Walk me through the process of calculating a service charge apportionment, explaining each step." This produces better results because it forces the AI to reason through the problem rather than jumping to a conclusion.

5. Assign a Role

"Act as a senior chartered surveyor reviewing this document." "Pretend you are explaining this to a first-year apprentice." Role-based prompts help Claude calibrate the depth and tone of its response.

Advanced Technique: Iterative Refinement

You rarely get the perfect output on the first try. Use follow-up messages to refine:

  • "That is good, but make it more formal."
  • "Can you expand on point 3?"
  • "Rewrite this but keep it under 100 words."
  • "That email is too aggressive. Soften the tone but keep the key message."

Analogy: Art Direction

Working with Claude is like being an art director. You do not need to be able to paint the picture yourself — you need to be able to clearly describe what you want, look at the first draft, and give precise feedback on what to change. The better your feedback, the better the final output.

What is Automation?

Doing Less Manual Work

Automation is the use of technology to perform tasks with minimal human intervention. Every time you set an out-of-office reply on your email, you are using a simple form of automation. The goal is to eliminate repetitive, low-value tasks so humans can focus on work that requires judgement, creativity, and relationships.

Two Types of Automation

Rule-Based Automation

"If X happens, do Y." Simple, predictable, and reliable. Examples: auto-forwarding emails, scheduled reports, calendar reminders, form auto-fill. These follow fixed rules and never deviate.

Property example: When a lease break date is 6 months away, automatically send a reminder email to the property manager.

AI-Powered Automation

"Understand this input and decide what to do." More flexible, can handle ambiguity, and improves over time. Examples: classifying incoming documents, drafting responses, analysing images.

Property example: An email arrives from a tenant. AI reads it, determines it is a maintenance request for a leaking roof, classifies it as urgent, creates a work order, and notifies the property manager — all automatically.

Analogy: The Assembly Line

Think of automation like a factory assembly line. Instead of one person building an entire car from scratch, each station handles one specific task. Rule-based automation is like a conveyor belt — it moves things along a fixed path. AI-powered automation is like adding a quality inspector at each station who can spot problems and make decisions about what to do next. The best systems combine both.

APIs Explained

How Software Talks to Software

An API (Application Programming Interface) is how different software systems communicate with each other. Every time you use an app on your phone that connects to a service (checking the weather, ordering food, paying someone), that app is using an API behind the scenes.

Analogy: The Restaurant Waiter

Imagine you are at a restaurant. You (the customer) want food from the kitchen. But you cannot walk into the kitchen yourself. The waiter takes your order (your "request"), carries it to the kitchen (the "server"), the kitchen prepares the food (processes the request), and the waiter brings it back to you (the "response"). An API is the waiter. It is the middleman that carries requests between two systems and brings back the results.

Why APIs Matter for Property

APIs are what allow us to connect different systems together. Without APIs, every piece of software would be an island. With APIs:

  • Xero can talk to your property management system to automatically reconcile payments
  • Google Calendar can be updated automatically when a new inspection is scheduled
  • Slack can receive notifications when a tenant submits a maintenance request
  • Stripe can process subscription payments for our SaaS products
  • Claude can be called by our applications to analyse documents or generate text

API Concepts in Plain English

Term Plain English Restaurant Analogy
Endpoint The specific address you send a request to Which counter you go to (bar, food, dessert)
Request Asking for something Placing your order
Response What you get back Your food arriving
API Key A password proving you are allowed to use the service Your table reservation number
Rate Limit How many requests you can make per minute The kitchen can only handle so many orders at once
Webhook The service notifies YOU when something happens (instead of you checking repeatedly) The waiter brings your food when it is ready, instead of you going to check every 2 minutes

MCP: Connecting AI to Everything

What is MCP?

The Model Context Protocol (MCP) is a standard created by Anthropic that allows AI models to connect to external tools and services. Without MCP, Claude is limited to text conversations. With MCP, Claude can interact with virtually any software system.

Analogy: Universal Adaptor

Think of MCP like a universal power adaptor when you travel abroad. Your laptop (Claude) has one type of plug. Each country (software service) has a different socket. MCP is the adaptor that lets your laptop plug into any socket in the world. Instead of building a custom connection for every single tool, MCP provides one standard way to connect Claude to anything.

What is Digital Transformation?

More Than Just "Going Digital"

Digital transformation is the process of fundamentally changing how a business operates by integrating technology into every area. It is not just about buying new software or having a website. It is about rethinking processes, culture, and business models with technology at the centre.

Putting a PDF on a website instead of posting a letter is digitisation. Completely redesigning the process so the letter is never needed in the first place — that is transformation.

The Digital Maturity Spectrum

Most property firms sit somewhere on this spectrum. Understanding where a firm is helps you understand what they need next.

Level Description Property Example

Level 3: Connected Systems linked together, some automation Property management software connected to accounting, automated reminders, cloud storage
Level 4: Intelligent AI and advanced automation, data-driven decisions AI-powered lease analysis, automated tenant comms, predictive maintenance, smart reporting
Level 5: Autonomous Self-optimising systems, AI agents acting independently AI agents handling routine property management tasks end-to-end, humans oversee and handle exceptions

Hillway operates at Level 4-5 internally and helps clients move from Levels 1-2 up to Levels 3-4. Most traditional property firms are at Level 2. The firms that get to Level 3-4 first will have a significant competitive advantage. This is exactly what Hillway Digital sells.

RICS AI Standard

Regulation is Coming

The Royal Institution of Chartered Surveyors (RICS) is introducing its first formal guidance on AI use in surveying practice, effective 9 March 2026. This is a landmark moment: it means AI is now officially part of how the profession is regulated.

Key Principles of the RICS AI Standard

While the full standard is detailed, the core principles are:

  • Transparency: Clients should be informed when AI is used in the delivery of professional services. You do not need to list every tool, but material use of AI should be disclosed
  • Professional responsibility: The surveyor remains responsible for the output, regardless of whether AI was used to generate it. You cannot blame the AI if something is wrong
  • Data protection: Client data used in AI systems must be handled in accordance with GDPR and RICS data protection requirements. You cannot paste confidential client data into a public AI tool without appropriate safeguards
  • Competence: Surveyors using AI must have sufficient understanding of the technology to use it responsibly. This is exactly why you are taking this course
  • Quality assurance: AI outputs must be reviewed and validated by a qualified professional before being relied upon or shared with clients

What This Means for You

As an apprentice surveyor working towards your APC, understanding AI governance is not optional — it is becoming a core competency. When you sit your APC assessment, examiners may ask about AI ethics, data protection, and professional responsibility in the context of technology use. Hillway's approach to AI already aligns with these principles, so by working here you are building the right habits from day one.
