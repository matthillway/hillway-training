Introduction to Property Management

What property management is, who is involved, the types of property you will manage, and the professional standards that govern everything we do.

1.1

What is Property Management?

The Core Definition

Property management is the day-to-day oversight of commercial or residential real estate on behalf of the property owner. As a managing agent, Hillway acts as the landlord's representative, ensuring the property is well-maintained, tenants are managed effectively, income is collected, and the asset retains or increases in value.

Think of it this way: the landlord owns the building, but they hire us to run it. We are the operational engine behind the investment. Our job is to protect the landlord's income stream, manage the physical condition of the asset, and deal with every stakeholder who interacts with it.

Property management sits within the broader field of real estate, alongside agency (buying/selling/letting), valuation, and building consultancy. As a chartered surveying practice, Hillway provides a range of these services, but property management is a core discipline that underpins everything else.

Why Property Management Matters

A building that is poorly managed loses tenants, loses value, and costs the landlord money. A building that is well managed does the opposite — it attracts quality tenants, generates stable income, appreciates in value, and creates a reputation that benefits the landlord's entire portfolio.

Consider a multi-let office building worth £5 million, generating £400,000 per annum in rent. If poor management causes a tenant paying £80,000 to vacate, the landlord loses not just the rent but also faces void costs (rates, service charge shortfall, marketing) that could total £30,000-£50,000 over a 12-month void period. Meanwhile, the capital value of the building drops because the income has dropped. This is why landlords pay management fees — the cost of good management is a fraction of the cost of bad management.

Property management is not glamorous work. It is detailed, relentless, and often thankless. But it is the foundation upon which every commercial property investment depends. Without competent management, even the best building in the best location will underperform.

RICS Guidance — Real Estate Management

The Property Management Lifecycle

Every property goes through a lifecycle, and a managing agent is involved at every stage:

Acquisition

The landlord buys the property. We may be asked to provide management input during due diligence — reviewing leases, assessing service charge budgets, identifying compliance issues, and estimating management costs.

Onboarding

We take over management. This involves establishing systems, meeting tenants, reviewing contracts, setting up financial accounts, and creating a property management plan.

Steady-State Management

The ongoing work — rent collection, service charge administration, maintenance, tenant liaison, lease events, compliance, and reporting. This is where you will spend most of your time.

Value Enhancement

Proactive management that increases the property's value — re-letting vacant units, negotiating lease renewals, improving energy efficiency, upgrading common areas, and reducing operating costs.

Disposal

The landlord sells the property. We assist by providing information to buyers, managing the transition to a new managing agent, and ensuring the handover is clean and complete.

1.2

The Managing Agent's Role and Responsibilities

Day-to-Day Responsibilities

As a managing agent, your day will involve a wide range of tasks. No two days are the same, which is part of what makes property management interesting. Here are the core responsibilities:

  • Rent collection — Ensuring tenants pay their rent on time and chasing arrears when they do not
  • Lease administration — Tracking lease events such as break clauses, rent reviews, and lease expiries
  • Service charge management — Preparing budgets, managing expenditure, and reconciling year-end accounts
  • Building maintenance — Organising reactive repairs and planned preventive maintenance
  • Tenant liaison — Being the first point of contact for tenant queries, complaints, and requests
  • Compliance — Ensuring the property meets health and safety, fire safety, and environmental regulations
  • Insurance — Arranging and managing buildings insurance and dealing with claims
  • Reporting — Providing landlords with regular updates on the property's performance
  • Contractor management — Appointing, supervising, and paying contractors and service providers
  • Lease enforcement — Ensuring tenants comply with their lease obligations (e.g. not making unauthorised alterations)

Duties of Care

As a managing agent, you owe duties of care to several parties simultaneously. This is one of the trickiest aspects of the job:

  • To your client (the landlord) — A contractual and fiduciary duty to act in their best interests, protect their income, and manage the property competently
  • To tenants — While they are not your client, you owe them a duty to manage the property fairly and in accordance with the lease. The RICS Code also requires you to treat tenants professionally
  • To the public — A statutory duty of care under health and safety legislation. Anyone who enters the building must be safe
  • To your profession — As employees of an RICS firm, you must uphold the standards of the profession. This includes acting with integrity, being competent, and providing a good service

Sometimes these duties conflict. For example, a landlord might want to minimise service charge expenditure (their duty to shareholders), but the building needs essential maintenance (your duty of care to occupiers). Navigating these conflicts is a key skill for a property manager.

Stakeholders You Will Work With

Property management involves balancing the needs and expectations of multiple parties. You will interact with all of these regularly:

Landlords / Property Owners

The Client

The landlord pays our fees and is our primary client. They may be a private individual, a property company, a pension fund, or an institutional investor. Their main interest is maximising income and protecting the capital value of their asset. We report to them and act on their instructions.

Tenants / Occupiers

The Day-to-Day Relationship

Tenants occupy the building under the terms of a lease. They pay rent and service charge. While the landlord is our client, tenants are the people we deal with most frequently. A good managing agent maintains professional, constructive relationships with tenants. Happy tenants stay longer and pay on time.

Contractors / Service Providers

The Delivery Team

Cleaners, security guards, M&E engineers, landscapers, lift maintenance companies — these are the people who physically look after the building. We appoint them, specify the work, monitor quality, and pay them (from the service charge). Managing contractors well is critical to a well-run property.

Other Professionals

The Advisory Network

Solicitors, accountants, insurance brokers, building surveyors, planning consultants, and letting agents. We coordinate with these professionals when specialist advice is needed. For example, a solicitor handles lease negotiations, an accountant audits service charge accounts, and a letting agent finds new tenants.

1.3

Types of Managed Property

Property Types at Hillway

Hillway manages a range of commercial property types. Each has its own characteristics, lease structures, and management challenges. Understanding these differences is essential because the way you manage a single-let industrial unit is fundamentally different from how you manage a multi-let office building.

Single-Let

One tenant, whole building

A single occupier leases the entire property. Management is simpler — there is no service charge to manage (the tenant handles everything under a Full Repairing and Insuring lease, known as an "FRI" lease). Your focus is rent collection, lease compliance, and the landlord-tenant relationship. Examples: a standalone warehouse let to Amazon, or a retail unit let to Tesco.

Multi-Let Office

Multiple tenants, shared services

Several tenants share a building, each leasing a floor or part of a floor. The landlord retains control of common areas (reception, lifts, WCs, car park) and provides shared services funded by a service charge. This is the most management-intensive property type. Examples: a city-centre office building with 6 tenants.

Retail Park / Shopping Centre

Retail units with common areas

Multiple retail tenants with shared car parking, landscaping, and estate roads. Management issues include car park maintenance, signage, opening hours, and managing the tenant mix. Footfall and the customer experience are important considerations. Examples: Meadowhall, a retail park on the edge of town.

Industrial Estate

Warehouses and distribution units

Industrial and logistics units, often on an estate with shared roads and drainage. Tenants tend to be more self-sufficient, but estate management (roads, landscaping, drainage, security) still requires active oversight. Yard areas and HGV access are common management issues.

Management Agreements

Before we start managing a property, we enter into a management agreement with the landlord. This is a formal contract that sets out:

  • Scope of services — Exactly what we will and will not do. Be very careful about scope creep
  • Authority levels — The expenditure limit we can approve without further instruction (e.g. up to £2,500 for emergency repairs)
  • Management fee — Typically 5% to 10% of rent collected, or a fixed annual fee. Multi-let properties command higher fees
  • Additional fees — For specific activities such as letting, rent reviews, licence to alter, or project management of major works
  • Notice period — Usually 3 to 6 months for either party to terminate
  • Professional indemnity — We must maintain PI insurance, typically £1 million minimum
  • Client money provisions — How we handle the landlord's money (covered in Day 3)

Always read the management agreement carefully. It defines the boundaries of your authority and your obligations. If something is not in the agreement, you should not be doing it without the landlord's specific instruction.

1.4

The RICS Property Management Code

Why RICS Standards Matter

As an RICS-regulated firm, Hillway is bound by the RICS Code of Practice for Service Charges in Commercial Property (3rd Edition) and the RICS Real Estate Management guidelines. These are not optional — they are mandatory for all RICS members and regulated firms. Breaching them can result in disciplinary action.

The RICS Code sets out the professional standards we must meet:

  • Transparency — Be open and honest with all parties. No hidden fees, no undisclosed conflicts of interest
  • Competence — Only take on work you are qualified and equipped to handle. Know your limits
  • Ethical behaviour — Act with integrity. Never take inducements or kickbacks from contractors
  • Client money protection — Handle all client funds strictly in accordance with RICS Client Money Handling rules
  • Fair treatment of tenants — Even though the landlord is our client, we must treat tenants fairly and proportionately
  • Record keeping — Maintain comprehensive records of all management activities, decisions, and communications

The Five RICS Ethical Standards

Every RICS member must uphold these five standards at all times. You should memorise them:

Act with Integrity

Be honest and straightforward in all that you do. Never mislead anyone, whether client, tenant, contractor, or the public. If you make a mistake, own it immediately.

Always Provide a High Standard of Service

Be diligent, competent, and timely. Respond to queries promptly. Meet your deadlines. Do what you say you will do.

Act in a Way That Promotes Trust in the Profession

Your actions reflect on Hillway and on the entire RICS profession. Behave in a way that upholds public confidence in chartered surveyors.

Treat Others with Respect

Treat everyone — clients, tenants, contractors, colleagues — with courtesy and professionalism. Never discriminate or bully.

Take Responsibility

Be accountable for your actions. If something goes wrong on your watch, take ownership and fix it. Do not pass the blame.

Conflicts of Interest

A conflict of interest arises when your personal interests, or the interests of another client, could compromise your ability to act in your current client's best interests. Common examples in property management:

  • Managing a property where you or a family member is a tenant
  • Recommending a contractor who is a personal friend or who offers you incentives
  • Acting for both the landlord and the tenant in a dispute
  • Managing competing properties for different landlords in the same market

If you identify a potential conflict, you must disclose it immediately to your line manager. RICS requires that conflicts are identified, disclosed, and managed — or that the conflicted party steps aside.

Lease Administration & Tenant Management

Understanding commercial leases, key clauses, the letting process, and how to manage tenant relationships professionally.

2.1

Reading and Understanding a Commercial Lease

What is a Lease?

A lease is a legal contract between a landlord and a tenant that grants the tenant exclusive possession of a property (or part of a property) for a specified period, in return for the payment of rent. In commercial property, leases are typically long, complex documents drafted by solicitors.

You do not need to be a solicitor to work with leases, but you must be able to read and understand the key provisions. Every management decision you make — from collecting rent to approving alterations to enforcing repair obligations — is governed by the lease. If you do not know what the lease says, you cannot manage the property properly.

A typical commercial lease will be 40-80 pages long and will cover the parties, the premises, the term, the rent, the service charge, the tenant's covenants, the landlord's covenants, and a range of miscellaneous provisions. It will be accompanied by plans showing the demised premises.

Key Lease Terms You Must Know

These are the fundamental terms that appear in every commercial lease:

  • Demise — The physical space being let. Defined by plans attached to the lease, including what is "inside" the demise (tenant's responsibility) and what is "outside" (landlord's/common areas)
  • Term — The length of the lease, e.g. 10 years from 1 January 2025. The "contractual term" is the full period; the tenant may also have security of tenure under the Landlord and Tenant Act 1954
  • Rent — The annual rent payable, usually paid quarterly in advance on the traditional English quarter days (25 March, 24 June, 29 September, 25 December) or monthly in advance
  • Rent review — A mechanism to adjust the rent periodically, typically every 5 years. Usually an upward-only review to open market rent
  • Break clause — An option for either the landlord, the tenant, or both to terminate the lease early, subject to conditions
  • Permitted use — What the tenant is allowed to use the premises for, defined by reference to the Town and Country Planning (Use Classes) Order
  • Tenant's covenants — The obligations the tenant agrees to (pay rent, repair, insure, not to assign without consent, etc.)
  • Landlord's covenants — The obligations the landlord agrees to (provide services, maintain common areas, insure the building)

Lease Event Diary Management

One of the most critical aspects of lease administration is tracking lease events — key dates in the lease that require action. Missing a lease event can have severe financial consequences for the landlord. For example:

  • Missing a rent review date may mean the landlord cannot increase the rent for another 5 years
  • Missing a break clause deadline may mean a tenant can leave when the landlord did not want them to, or cannot leave when they need to
  • Missing a lease expiry means the lease may continue under the Landlord and Tenant Act 1954 on the same terms, preventing the landlord from re-letting at a higher rent

At Hillway, every lease event is entered into our property management system with cascading reminders: 12 months, 6 months, 3 months, and 1 month before the event. This gives the landlord enough time to take advice and decide on a course of action.

2.2

Key Lease Clauses Surveyors Must Know

User Clause

What the tenant can do

Restricts the use of the premises to a specific purpose (e.g. "offices within Use Class E(g)(i)"). Changing use requires landlord's consent and possibly planning permission. A restrictive user clause can make the property harder to re-let if the tenant leaves.

Alterations Clause

What the tenant can change

Defines what alterations the tenant can make. Usually: no structural alterations; non-structural alterations with landlord's consent (not to be unreasonably withheld). The tenant typically needs to apply for a "licence to alter" — a formal document that records the permitted works and any conditions (e.g. reinstatement at lease end).

Alienation Clause

Assigning and subletting

"Alienation" means transferring the lease to someone else. Most leases allow assignment (transferring the whole lease) and subletting (letting part or all of the premises to a sub-tenant) with the landlord's prior written consent. The landlord can impose conditions, such as requiring an Authorised Guarantee Agreement (AGA) from the outgoing tenant.

Repair Clause

Who fixes what

Defines the repairing obligations of the tenant and the landlord. In a multi-let building, the tenant typically repairs the interior of their demise, and the landlord repairs the structure, exterior, and common areas (recovered through the service charge). The standard of repair required is usually "good and tenantable repair and condition".

Insurance Clause

Building insurance arrangements

In a multi-let building, the landlord insures the building and recovers the premium from tenants through the service charge or as "insurance rent". The lease will specify who arranges insurance, on what terms, and how the premium is recovered. Tenants are usually required to notify the landlord of anything that might affect the insurance.

Service Charge Clause

Shared costs

Sets out what the landlord can include in the service charge, how costs are apportioned between tenants, and the procedures for budgeting, collecting, and reconciling the charge. This clause is critical — it defines the scope of recoverable expenditure and protects both landlord and tenant.

The lease is the rule book. Every question about what the landlord can do, what the tenant must do, and who pays for what can be answered by reading the lease. If you cannot find the answer in the lease, take legal advice before acting.

2.3

The Letting Process

From Void to Occupied

When a unit becomes vacant, the managing agent works with the letting agent (which may be a colleague at Hillway) to find a new tenant as quickly as possible. Void periods cost the landlord money — no rent, plus they may have to pay empty rates, maintain the property, and fund the service charge shortfall. The letting process follows a clear sequence:

Marketing

Prepare marketing materials, instruct agents, list the property on commercial property portals (e.g. CoStar, Rightmove Commercial, EGi), and erect a "To Let" board. The managing agent provides the letting agent with property information including floor areas, EPC rating, service charge budget, and available fit-out.

Viewings and Negotiations

The letting agent conducts viewings and fields enquiries. Interested parties make offers. Negotiations cover rent, lease length, rent-free period, break clauses, and any contribution to fit-out costs. The managing agent advises the landlord on the strength of each offer.

Heads of Terms

Once terms are agreed, they are documented in "Heads of Terms" (HoT) — a non-binding summary of the agreed deal. This is sent to both parties' solicitors to instruct the lease drafting. Heads of Terms typically cover: parties, premises, term, rent, rent-free, break, service charge, repair obligations, and any special conditions.

Agreement to Lease

Solicitors draft and negotiate the lease. This can take weeks or months depending on complexity. The managing agent may need to provide information during this process (e.g. service charge budgets, insurance details, schedules of condition). They should chase progress regularly to avoid delays.

Completion and Handover

The lease is signed and the tenant takes possession. The managing agent handles the practical handover: keys, access cards, meter readings, utility transfers, fire safety briefing, and an introduction to the building and its systems. First impressions matter — a smooth handover sets the tone for the entire tenancy.

Tenant Onboarding

When a new tenant moves in, the managing agent should provide:

  • A tenant handbook — covering building rules, emergency procedures, contractor access, waste disposal, parking, and key contacts
  • Emergency contact details — out-of-hours numbers, fire assembly points, and evacuation procedures
  • Service charge information — current budget, payment schedule, and what is included
  • Building access — keys, access cards, security codes, and any restrictions on hours
  • A schedule of condition — documenting the state of the premises at move-in (critical for dilapidations at lease end)

Dealing with Difficult Tenants

Most tenants are reasonable, but you will encounter difficult situations. The key principles are:

  • Stay professional — Never lose your temper or respond emotionally. Always communicate in writing to create a record
  • Know the lease — Every dispute comes back to the lease. If the tenant is in breach, cite the specific clause. If they are not in breach, you may need to find a different approach
  • Escalate appropriately — Start with informal discussion, then formal letter, then involve your line manager, then legal advice. Do not jump straight to threats
  • Document everything — Keep a file note of every conversation, every email, and every action. This protects you and the landlord if the dispute escalates
  • Be fair — RICS requires fair treatment. Even if the tenant is being unreasonable, you must respond proportionately

Question 1 of 8
In lease terminology, what does "the demise" refer to?
The "demise" (or "demised premises") is the physical space being let to the tenant. It is defined by plans attached to the lease, which show exactly what is inside the demise (the tenant's exclusive area) and what is outside it (common areas, structure, etc.). Getting the demise right is critical for understanding repair obligations and service charge apportionment.
Question 2 of 8
Which of these is NOT a traditional English quarter day for rent payment?
The four traditional English quarter days are: 25 March (Lady Day), 24 June (Midsummer Day), 29 September (Michaelmas), and 25 December (Christmas Day). "1 October" is not a quarter day. Many modern leases now use monthly rent payment instead, but the quarter day system remains common in commercial property.
Question 3 of 8
What is the typical consequence of missing a rent review date?
Missing a rent review date can mean the landlord cannot increase the rent for another 5 years (until the next review). While some leases have "time not of the essence" provisions that offer some protection, others are strict. This is why lease event diary management with cascading reminders is so important — it is one of the managing agent's most critical administrative tasks.
Question 4 of 8
What formal document does a tenant need before making non-structural alterations?
A "licence to alter" is the formal legal document that records the landlord's consent to the tenant's proposed alterations. It sets out what works are permitted, any conditions (such as using approved contractors or reinstating at lease end), and often requires the tenant to pay the landlord's legal and surveying costs. Without a licence, the tenant is in breach of the lease.
Question 5 of 8
What does "alienation" mean in the context of a commercial lease?
Alienation refers to the transfer of the tenant's interest in the lease, either by assignment (transferring the whole lease to a new tenant) or subletting (the tenant remains on the lease but lets all or part of the premises to a sub-tenant). Most leases require the landlord's prior written consent for alienation, and the landlord can impose reasonable conditions.
Question 6 of 8
What are "Heads of Terms" in the letting process?
Heads of Terms (HoT) is a non-binding summary of the deal agreed between landlord and tenant. It covers the key commercial terms — parties, premises, term, rent, rent-free period, break clauses, and service charge. It is sent to both parties' solicitors to instruct the drafting of the formal lease. While non-binding, it sets clear expectations for the legal process.
Question 7 of 8
Why is a schedule of condition important at the start of a tenancy?
A schedule of condition records the property's state at the start of the tenancy. At lease end, it is compared against the actual condition to identify dilapidations — items of disrepair that the tenant is responsible for. Without a schedule of condition, disputes about the starting condition can be very difficult to resolve. It protects both landlord and tenant.
Question 8 of 8
What is the correct escalation sequence when dealing with a tenant in breach of their lease?
The correct approach is graduated escalation: start with an informal discussion to understand the situation, then send a formal letter citing the specific lease clause being breached, then involve your line manager if the breach continues, and finally take legal advice about enforcement options. Jumping straight to threats or legal action is unprofessional and can damage the landlord-tenant relationship unnecessarily.

Day 3 — Wednesday

Rent Collection, Arrears & Financial Management

The money side of property management: how rent is collected, what happens when tenants do not pay, client money rules, and financial reporting to landlords.

3.1

The Rent Collection Cycle

Why Rent Collection Matters

Rent is the landlord's income. Collecting it efficiently and on time is the single most important thing a managing agent does. A landlord will tolerate many things, but poor rent collection is not one of them. If tenants are not paying and you are not chasing, you will lose the instruction.

The rent collection cycle is a repeating process that happens every quarter (or every month for monthly-paying tenants). It requires discipline, attention to detail, and consistent follow-through.

Issue Rent Demands

Before each quarter day, issue formal rent demands to all tenants. The demand must state the amount due, the period it covers, and the date by which payment must be received. Under the Landlord and Tenant Act 1987 (for certain property types), a valid rent demand must include the landlord's name and address.

Monitor Payments

On and after the quarter day, monitor incoming payments. Match each payment to the correct tenant and the correct property. Check that the amount matches what is due — tenants sometimes underpay (deliberately or by error). Flag any discrepancies immediately.

Chase Arrears

If a tenant has not paid within 7 days of the due date, send a reminder. If no payment is received within 14 days, make a phone call. If still unpaid after 21 days, send a formal letter. The key is to be prompt and consistent — letting arrears drift is the most common management failing.

Reconcile and Report

At the end of each collection period, produce a rent schedule showing what was due, what was received, and what remains outstanding. Report this to the landlord along with commentary on any arrears and the actions being taken. Transparency is essential.

Quarter Days vs Monthly Collection

Traditionally, commercial rent is paid quarterly in advance on the English quarter days. However, there is a growing trend towards monthly collection, particularly for smaller tenants or during difficult economic periods.

Method Advantages Disadvantages
Quarterly Fewer transactions, lower admin cost, traditional market practice Large lump sums harder for tenants to manage, bigger exposure if they default
Monthly Easier cashflow for tenants, earlier detection of problems, lower risk per payment 12x more transactions, higher admin burden, less traditional
3.2

Arrears Management and Enforcement

Credit Control Escalation

When a tenant falls into arrears, you must follow a structured credit control process. The key is to act quickly and decisively — the longer arrears persist, the harder they are to recover. Here is the standard escalation:

  • Day 7 — First reminder letter or email. Polite but firm. State the amount due and request immediate payment
  • Day 14 — Telephone call to the tenant. Try to understand why they have not paid. Agree a payment plan if appropriate
  • Day 21 — Formal written demand. Warn of potential consequences (interest, forfeiture, CRAR). Copy to the landlord for instruction
  • Day 28 — Report to landlord with recommendation. Options include: continuing to negotiate, instructing solicitors, pursuing CRAR, or serving a statutory demand

Enforcement Options

When negotiation fails, the landlord has several legal remedies for recovering unpaid rent:

CRAR

Commercial Rent Arrears Recovery

Replaced the ancient remedy of "distress". An enforcement agent (bailiff) attends the premises and seizes the tenant's goods to sell and recover the arrears. Can only be used for pure rent arrears (not service charge or insurance rent). Requires a minimum of 7 days' rent to be outstanding and 7 days' notice to the tenant. A powerful but aggressive tool — use as a last resort.

Forfeiture

Terminating the lease

The landlord can forfeit (terminate) the lease for non-payment of rent, provided the lease contains a forfeiture clause (almost all do). This is done by peaceable re-entry or by court proceedings. The tenant has the right to apply for relief from forfeiture (essentially a second chance to pay). Forfeiture is a nuclear option — it ends the income stream entirely and should only be used when the tenant is clearly unable to pay.

Statutory Demand

Insolvency threat

A formal demand under the Insolvency Act 1986 requiring payment within 21 days. If the tenant fails to pay or set aside the demand, the landlord can petition for the tenant's winding up (company) or bankruptcy (individual). This is a serious step and can trigger a defensive response from the tenant, so it should be used strategically.

County Court Claim

Debt recovery through courts

A straightforward debt claim through the courts. Relatively slow (can take months) but results in a county court judgment (CCJ) that appears on the tenant's credit record. Can be enforced through various means including attachment of earnings, charging orders, or writ of control.

Bad Debt Provisioning

In financial reporting to landlords, it is good practice to include a bad debt provision — an estimate of the rent that may not be recovered. This gives the landlord a realistic picture of their expected income rather than an optimistic one. A typical approach:

  • 0-30 days overdue — No provision (normal payment cycle)
  • 31-60 days overdue — 25% provision
  • 61-90 days overdue — 50% provision
  • 90+ days overdue — 75-100% provision
  • Tenant in administration/liquidation — 100% provision
3.3

Client Money Handling and Financial Reporting

RICS Client Money Rules

As a managing agent, you handle other people's money — rent collected on behalf of landlords, service charge funds held on behalf of tenants, and deposits. This is client money, and RICS has strict rules about how it must be handled:

  • Separate bank accounts — Client money must be held in designated client accounts, completely separate from the firm's own money. Never mix client and office funds
  • Proper records — Maintain detailed records showing exactly how much is held for each client at any time. Reconcile client accounts at least monthly
  • Prompt transfer — Rent collected should be transferred to the landlord promptly (usually within an agreed timeframe, often 5-10 working days)
  • Interest — Any interest earned on client money belongs to the client, not to the firm (unless otherwise agreed in the management agreement)
  • Annual reporting — The firm must report annually on its client money handling and may be subject to inspection by RICS
  • Client Money Protection (CMP) scheme — RICS requires firms to participate in a CMP scheme that protects client money if the firm fails

Getting client money handling wrong is one of the most serious breaches an RICS firm can commit. It can result in loss of RICS registration, which effectively ends your ability to practice as a chartered surveying firm.

Financial Reporting to Landlords

Landlords expect regular financial reports on their properties. The frequency and detail depend on the management agreement, but typically includes:

  • Rent schedule — A list of all tenants, their rent, what has been collected, and what is outstanding. Usually produced quarterly
  • Cashflow statement — Money in (rent, service charge) and money out (contractor payments, insurance, management fees). Shows the net position
  • Service charge report — Budget vs actual expenditure, showing whether the service charge is on track or likely to be over/under budget
  • Management report — Narrative commentary on the property's performance, any issues, and actions taken. This is where you demonstrate your value as a managing agent
  • Arrears report — Detail on any outstanding rent, the age of the debt, and the actions being taken to recover it

VAT and Commercial Property

VAT is a complex area in commercial property. The key concepts you need to understand:

  • Option to tax — A landlord can "opt to tax" a commercial property, which means VAT at 20% is charged on the rent. This benefits the landlord if they want to recover input VAT on costs (e.g. refurbishment). Most commercial landlords opt to tax
  • Exempt supplies — If the property is not opted to tax, the rent is exempt from VAT. This means the landlord cannot recover input VAT on costs but the tenant does not pay VAT on rent (beneficial for tenants who cannot recover VAT, such as banks and insurance companies)
  • Service charge VAT — If the property is opted to tax, VAT is also charged on the service charge. The service charge budget should be set net of VAT with VAT added on top
  • Transfer of Going Concern (TOGC) — When a tenanted property is sold, the sale may be treated as a TOGC for VAT purposes, meaning no VAT is charged on the sale price. Strict conditions apply

You will not be expected to handle VAT compliance yourself (that is the accountant's job), but you need to understand these concepts to make sense of financial reports and to correctly raise invoices and rent demands.

4.1

Service Charges in Depth

What is a Service Charge?

A service charge is the mechanism by which a landlord recovers the costs of providing services and maintaining the common areas of a multi-let property. The landlord incurs the costs (cleaning, security, maintenance, insurance, management fees) and recovers them from tenants in proportion to their occupation.

The service charge is governed by three things: the lease (which defines what costs can be recovered and how they are apportioned), the RICS Professional Statement on Service Charges in Commercial Property (3rd Edition) (which sets out best practice), and good professional judgement.

Service charge management is one of the most technically demanding aspects of property management. It requires budgeting skills, financial management, procurement expertise, and the ability to communicate complex financial information clearly to tenants. Getting it wrong creates disputes, damages relationships, and can result in RICS complaints.

The RICS Professional Statement (3rd Edition)

The RICS Professional Statement is mandatory for all RICS members and firms. Key principles include:

  • Transparency — Tenants have a right to understand what they are paying for and why. Provide clear budgets and detailed year-end accounts
  • Timeliness — Issue budgets before the service charge year, interim accounts at mid-year, and final accounts within 4 months of year-end
  • Reasonableness — All expenditure must be reasonable and in the interests of good estate management. Gold-plating is not acceptable
  • Consultation — For significant expenditure, tenants should be consulted in advance (this is mandatory in residential, best practice in commercial)
  • Certification — Year-end accounts should be certified by an independent accountant for larger service charges

Budget Preparation

The service charge budget is prepared before the start of each service charge year. It estimates the total expenditure for the year across all cost categories. A well-prepared budget should:

  • Be based on actual costs from previous years, adjusted for inflation and known changes
  • Include all recoverable costs — do not leave items out to make the budget look lower, only to catch up in the reconciliation
  • Be benchmarked against similar properties to ensure costs are competitive
  • Include a management fee (typically 10-15% of the service charge total or a fixed amount)
  • Allow for a contingency (usually 2.5-5%) for unexpected costs

Typical Service Charge Cost Categories

Category Examples Typical % of Total
Mechanical & Electrical HVAC maintenance, lift maintenance, electrical testing, BMS 25-35%
Cleaning Common area cleaning, window cleaning, waste management 15-20%
Security Manned guarding, CCTV, access control, out-of-hours patrols 10-15%
Utilities Common area electricity, gas, water 10-15%
Repairs & Maintenance Reactive repairs, decorations, fabric maintenance 10-15%

Apportionment Methods

When multiple tenants share service charge costs, the landlord must apportion the total cost between them fairly. The three main methods are:

Floor Area

Most common method

Each tenant pays in proportion to the floor area they occupy (measured in sq ft or sq m). If a tenant occupies 20% of the building's total lettable area, they pay 20% of the service charge. This is the simplest and most transparent method. It is defined in the lease and does not change unless the building layout changes.

Rateable Value

Based on VOA assessment

Each tenant pays in proportion to the rateable value of their demise (as assessed by the Valuation Office Agency for business rates). This can be more equitable than floor area for mixed-use buildings (e.g. ground floor retail + upper floor offices) because it reflects the different values of different spaces. However, it changes when rateable values are reassessed.

Weighted Apportionment

A more sophisticated approach that applies different weights to different cost categories. For example, ground floor retail tenants might pay a higher proportion of car park costs (because their customers use the car park) but a lower proportion of lift costs (because they do not use the lifts). This is the fairest method but the most complex to administer.

Sinking Funds and Reserve Funds

A sinking fund (also called a reserve fund) is money collected through the service charge and set aside for future major expenditure — such as replacing the roof, renewing the lifts, or redecorating the exterior. Without a sinking fund, these large costs hit tenants in a single year, which can be very unpopular.

The RICS Professional Statement encourages the use of sinking funds. They should be:

  • Based on a planned maintenance programme with costed projections
  • Held in a separate designated bank account (not mixed with revenue service charge funds)
  • Transparent — tenants should know what the fund is for and how much has been accumulated
  • Fair — contributions should reflect the expected timing and cost of future works

Year-End Reconciliation

At the end of each service charge year, the actual expenditure is compared to the budget. The managing agent produces a reconciliation showing:

  • Budget vs actual for each cost category
  • Total on-account payments received from each tenant
  • Whether each tenant has overpaid or underpaid
  • Balancing charges (additional invoices) or credits (refunds) due

The reconciliation should be issued within 4 months of the service charge year-end. Delays frustrate tenants and can make it harder to recover balancing charges. The accounts should be certified by an independent accountant for larger service charges.

4.2

Building Insurance

Building Insurance Basics

Buildings insurance covers the physical structure of the property against damage from insured perils — fire, flood, storm, subsidence, impact, explosion, and other specified risks. In a multi-let building, the landlord arranges insurance and recovers the premium from tenants (either through the service charge or as a separate "insurance rent").

Key Insurance Concepts

  • Reinstatement value — The cost of completely rebuilding the property from scratch, including demolition, debris removal, professional fees, and inflation allowance. This is NOT the market value — it is the construction cost. A building worth £5 million on the market might have a reinstatement value of £8 million
  • Declared value — The reinstatement value declared to the insurer at renewal. If this is too low (underinsurance), the insurer may apply "average" and only pay a proportion of any claim. Review annually
  • Subrogation — The insurer's right to pursue a third party who caused the loss. For example, if a contractor's negligence causes a fire, the insurer pays the claim and then sues the contractor to recover the money
  • Excess — The amount the policyholder must pay towards each claim before the insurer starts paying. Typically £250-£1,000 for buildings insurance
  • Loss of rent cover — Covers the landlord's lost rental income if the building is damaged and unoccupiable. Usually for a period of 2-3 years

Insurance Rent Recovery

The lease will define how the insurance premium is recovered from tenants. There are two common approaches:

  • Insurance rent — A separate charge, invoiced directly to each tenant alongside the rent. The tenant pays their apportioned share of the premium. This keeps insurance visible and separate from the service charge
  • Through the service charge — The insurance premium is included as a line item in the service charge budget. Simpler to administer but less transparent

Whichever method is used, the managing agent must ensure that the premium is recovered in full and that any change in premium (e.g. due to claims experience or market conditions) is communicated to tenants promptly.

Claims Handling Process

When building damage occurs, the managing agent's role in the claims process is:

  • Immediate response — Make the building safe. Prevent further damage. Arrange emergency repairs if necessary
  • Notify the insurer — Report the claim promptly with full details: what happened, when, where, and the estimated cost. Provide photographs
  • Appoint loss adjuster — For larger claims, the insurer will appoint a loss adjuster to assess the damage and agree the claim amount. Cooperate fully and provide all requested information
  • Manage the reinstatement — Instruct contractors to carry out repairs. Keep detailed records of costs. Ensure works are completed to a satisfactory standard
  • Settle the claim — Agree the final claim amount with the insurer. Ensure the landlord is not out of pocket. Recover any excess from the responsible party if applicable

5.1

Property Inspections and Maintenance

Property Inspections

Regular property inspections are the foundation of good building management. They allow you to identify maintenance issues before they become serious problems, verify that contractors are performing to standard, and check that tenants are complying with their lease obligations.

As a managing agent, you should aim to inspect each property at least quarterly. Some properties — particularly those with intensive public use such as retail centres — may need monthly inspections. Every inspection should be documented with a written report and photographs.

Inspection Methodology

A structured approach to inspections ensures nothing is missed:

  • External areas first — Car park, landscaping, signage, building exterior, roof (if safely accessible), drainage, boundary fences
  • Common areas — Reception, lifts, stairwells, corridors, WCs, plant rooms, bin stores
  • Tenant demises — Where access is available, check for lease breaches (unauthorised alterations, subletting, signage, storage in fire escape routes)
  • Building systems — Check that fire alarm panels show normal, emergency lighting is operational, HVAC is running correctly, no water leaks visible
  • Health and safety — Fire escape routes clear, fire extinguishers in date, trip hazards, asbestos labels intact, PPE requirements being followed

Photograph everything that needs attention. A photo taken during an inspection is worth a thousand words in a contractor brief or an insurance claim.

Planned Preventive Maintenance (PPM)

PPM is a schedule of regular maintenance tasks designed to keep building systems operating efficiently and extend their lifespan. It is always cheaper and less disruptive to maintain equipment properly than to wait for it to break down.

System Frequency Typical Tasks
HVAC Quarterly + annual Filter changes, refrigerant checks, ductwork inspections, AHU servicing

Reactive Maintenance and Emergency Procedures

Not everything can be planned. Reactive maintenance deals with unexpected breakdowns and urgent repairs. The key is to have clear procedures:

  • Prioritise by urgency — Emergency (respond 2-4 hours): burst pipe, fire damage, security breach. Urgent (respond 24 hours): heating failure, lift breakdown. Routine (respond 5 working days): faulty door closer, minor cosmetic repair
  • Pre-approved contractors — Maintain a list of vetted contractors for common emergencies: plumbers, electricians, locksmiths, glaziers, drainage engineers
  • Communicate immediately — Tell affected tenants what happened, what you are doing, and when to expect resolution. Silence creates frustration
  • Document everything — Photograph the issue, log the timeline, record costs. Critical for insurance claims and service charge accounting

Contractor Procurement and Management

Selecting and managing contractors is a core competency. The RICS Professional Statement requires that procurement is transparent, competitive, and in the best interests of good estate management:

  • Competitive tendering — For contracts above an agreed threshold (e.g. £5,000), obtain at least 3 competitive quotes. Evaluate on quality, experience, and price — not price alone
  • Due diligence — Check insurance, health and safety records, references, and financial standing before appointing
  • Clear specifications — Write detailed specifications so all bidders are pricing the same work. Vague specs lead to disputes
  • Performance monitoring — Regular KPI reviews, site inspections, and tenant feedback. Poor contractors should be warned and replaced if they do not improve
  • No conflicts of interest — Never accept gifts, incentives, or kickbacks from contractors. RICS ethical standards are absolute on this point

Dilapidations Overview

Dilapidations refers to the breaches of a tenant's repairing and other obligations under the lease. It becomes relevant when a lease is coming to an end:

  • Interim dilapidations — Served during the lease term when the tenant is not maintaining the property. A schedule of dilapidations is served listing the specific breaches and requiring the tenant to remedy them within a reasonable timeframe
  • Terminal dilapidations — Served at or shortly after the end of the lease. A detailed schedule lists all items of disrepair, want of decoration, and reinstatement of alterations. The landlord claims the cost of putting the property back into the condition required by the lease
  • Diminution in value — Section 18(1) of the Landlord and Tenant Act 1927 caps a dilapidations claim at the amount by which the landlord's interest has been diminished. In practice, this means the claim cannot exceed the difference in value between the property in repair and the property in its actual condition

Dilapidations is a specialist area usually handled by building surveyors (who prepare the schedule) and solicitors (who handle the legal aspects). As a property manager, your role is to flag potential dilapidations issues early, ensure schedules of condition are in place, and coordinate the process as lease end approaches.

5.2

Health & Safety Compliance

Your Legal Obligations

Health and safety is not optional. As the managing agent, you have a duty of care to everyone who enters the building. Getting this wrong can result in criminal prosecution, not just civil liability. The Health and Safety at Work etc. Act 1974 and its associated regulations place clear duties on those who control premises.

Fire Safety

Regulatory Reform (Fire Safety) Order 2005

The managing agent is typically the "Responsible Person" for fire safety in common areas. You must ensure a current fire risk assessment is in place, that it is reviewed regularly, and that its recommendations are implemented. Fire detection systems, emergency lighting, fire doors, and escape routes must all be maintained. After Grenfell, fire safety compliance is scrutinised more closely than ever.

Asbestos

Control of Asbestos Regulations 2012

If the building was constructed before 2000, it is likely to contain asbestos. The "duty holder" (usually the managing agent for common areas) must have an asbestos management survey, maintain an asbestos register, ensure anyone working on the building is aware of asbestos locations, and monitor the condition of asbestos-containing materials. Disturbing asbestos without proper controls is a criminal offence.

Legionella

HSE Approved Code of Practice L8

Legionella bacteria can grow in water systems (cooling towers, hot and cold water tanks, showers) and cause Legionnaires' disease — a potentially fatal form of pneumonia. The managing agent must ensure a legionella risk assessment is in place and that water systems are monitored and treated regularly. Monthly temperature checks and quarterly inspections are typical.

CDM Regulations

Construction (Design and Management) 2015

The CDM Regulations apply whenever construction or maintenance work is carried out. As the managing agent commissioning the work, you may be the "Client" under CDM and have duties to ensure the work is planned and carried out safely. For projects with more than one contractor, a Principal Designer and Principal Contractor must be appointed.

Other Key Compliance Areas

  • LOLER (Lifting Operations and Lifting Equipment Regulations 1998) — Lifts must be thoroughly examined every 6 months by a competent person (typically an insurance engineer). The examination report must be kept on file
  • Electrical safety — Fixed wire testing (EICR) every 5 years. PAT testing for portable appliances in common areas annually. Electrical installation certificates for any new work
  • Gas safety — Annual gas safety checks on any gas appliances in common areas. Gas Safe registered engineers only
  • Working at height — Any maintenance work involving ladders, scaffolding, or roof access must comply with the Work at Height Regulations 2005
  • Occupiers' liability — The Occupiers' Liability Acts 1957 and 1984 impose a duty of care on those who control premises to ensure visitors and even trespassers are reasonably safe

Environmental Management

Environmental compliance is increasingly important in property management:

  • EPC ratings — Every commercial property needs an Energy Performance Certificate when let or sold. The Minimum Energy Efficiency Standards (MEES) currently require a minimum rating of E (from April 2023). The government has proposed increasing this to C by 2027 and B by 2030
  • MEES compliance — If a property falls below the minimum EPC rating, it cannot be legally let. The managing agent must monitor EPC ratings and advise landlords on improvement works needed
  • Sustainability — Increasingly, tenants want sustainable buildings. LED lighting, smart building controls, renewable energy, EV charging, and waste recycling all contribute to a property's sustainability credentials and can help attract and retain quality tenants
  • Net Zero targets — Many institutional landlords have committed to net zero carbon by 2030 or 2050. As managing agents, we need to help them get there by reducing energy consumption and improving building efficiency

What does "diminution in value" cap in a dilapidations claim?
Section 18(1) of the Landlord and Tenant Act 1927 caps a dilapidations claim at the amount by which the landlord's interest has been diminished in value. In practice, this means the claim cannot exceed the difference between the property's value in its repaired state and its actual condition. For example, if repairs would cost £200,000 but the building will be demolished anyway, the diminution in value might be zero — so the landlord's claim is nil.
Question 10 of 10
How often must lifts be thoroughly examined under LOLER regulations?
Under LOLER (Lifting Operations and Lifting Equipment Regulations 1998), lifts must be thoroughly examined every 6 months by a competent person — typically an insurance engineer. This is a statutory requirement, not optional. The examination report must be kept on file and any defects addressed promptly. Note that this thorough examination is separate from the regular monthly maintenance carried out by the lift contractor.
