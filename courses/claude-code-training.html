Day 1: Understanding AI Assistants

What AI is, how it works, and why Claude is our tool of choice

What is AI? A Practical Introduction

Forget the science fiction

When people hear "artificial intelligence", they often think of robots or sentient computers from films. The reality is far more practical. AI, in the way we use it at Hillway, is software that can understand and generate human language, analyse documents, and help with tasks that would normally require significant human time and effort.

Think of it this way: a calculator helps you do maths faster. AI helps you do thinking tasks faster — reading, writing, summarising, analysing, and communicating.

Types of AI you already use

You interact with AI every day without realising it:

  • Predictive text on your phone — suggests the next word as you type
  • Spam filters in your email — learn what is junk and what is important
  • Sat nav routing — analyses traffic patterns to find the best route
  • Netflix/Spotify recommendations — learns what you like and suggests more
  • Voice assistants (Siri, Alexa) — understand spoken language

The AI we use at Hillway is a more advanced version of these ideas, specifically a Large Language Model (LLM). It specialises in understanding and generating text.

Why this matters for property

As a surveyor, a huge part of your job involves text: reading leases, writing reports, drafting emails, analysing comparable evidence, preparing fee proposals, and communicating with clients. AI is exceptionally good at all of these tasks. It does not replace your professional judgement — it amplifies it.

2

How Large Language Models Work

The autocomplete analogy

Start with something you already know. When you type a message on your phone and it suggests the next word, that is a simple language model. It has learned from billions of text messages that after "See you" the next word is often "later" or "tomorrow".

A Large Language Model like Claude works on exactly the same principle, but at an enormously larger scale. Instead of predicting one word from a short message, it has been trained on a vast amount of text (books, articles, websites, code) and can generate entire paragraphs, analyses, and documents — all by predicting what comes next, one piece at a time.

Key Concept: Tokens

What are tokens?

AI models do not read words the way you do. They break text into smaller pieces called tokens. A token is roughly three-quarters of a word. So "property management" is about 3 tokens, and a full page of text is roughly 400 tokens.

Why does this matter? Because there is a limit to how many tokens the model can process at once — this is called the context window. Think of it as the model's "working memory". Claude can handle approximately 200,000 tokens at once, which is roughly 500 pages of text. That is enough to read an entire lease and all supporting documents in one go.

Key Concept: Inference

What happens when you ask Claude a question?

When you send a message to Claude, a process called inference takes place. The model takes your input (the prompt), processes it against everything it learned during training, and generates a response token by token. This is why responses appear to stream in word by word — the model is literally generating each piece sequentially.

This is not a search engine looking up answers in a database. Claude is generating its response fresh each time based on the patterns it learned during training.

Important limitation: the knowledge cutoff

Claude's training data has a cutoff date. It does not know about events, legislation changes, or market data after that date unless you provide the information in your prompt. For property work, this means you should always provide current market evidence, recent case law, or updated regulations rather than asking Claude to recall them from memory.

Claude by Anthropic

Who makes Claude?

Anthropic is an AI safety company founded in 2021 by former members of OpenAI, including Dario and Daniela Amodei. Their core mission is to build AI systems that are safe, beneficial, and understandable. This safety-first approach is what sets Claude apart from competitors.

Constitutional AI: why Claude behaves differently

Claude is trained using a method called Constitutional AI. Rather than relying solely on human reviewers to judge every response, Claude is given a set of principles (a "constitution") that guide its behaviour. These principles include:

  • Be helpful, harmless, and honest
  • Do not assist with illegal or harmful activities
  • Acknowledge uncertainty rather than guess
  • Respect privacy and confidentiality
  • Be transparent about being an AI

In practice, this means Claude is less likely to make up information, more willing to say "I do not know", and more careful about sensitive topics. For professional work, this reliability is essential.

Understanding context windows and conversation memory

Within a single conversation, Claude remembers everything you have said. You can reference earlier messages, build on previous answers, and refine outputs iteratively. However:

  • Each conversation is separate — Claude does not remember your last chat when you start a new one (on claude.ai).
  • The context window is finite — Very long conversations may lose early context. For critical work, keep conversations focused on one topic.
  • You can paste documents — Copy and paste lease clauses, reports, or data directly into the chat. Claude processes them immediately.

Safety, Ethics & Responsible AI Use

The golden rule of AI at Hillway

AI is a tool, not a colleague. It produces drafts, not final products. Every piece of AI-generated content must be reviewed by a qualified human before it leaves the firm. This is not optional — it is a professional and regulatory requirement.

What AI does well

  • Drafting text that you then review and refine
  • Analysing large documents quickly (finding key clauses in a 200-page lease)
  • Summarising complex information into plain English
  • Generating first drafts of emails, reports, and proposals
  • Structuring your thoughts and organising research

What AI does NOT do well

  • Professional judgement — it cannot value a property or give a professional opinion
  • Guaranteeing accuracy of facts, figures, or legal citations
  • Understanding nuance that requires local market knowledge
  • Replacing the need for site inspections and physical observation
  • Confidential reasoning that accounts for relationships and politics

AI will not replace surveyors. But surveyors who use AI will replace those who do not.

— A useful way to think about it

Ethical use at Hillway

  • Transparency — If a client asks whether AI was used in preparing work, always be honest
  • Verification — Never send AI output directly to a client without human review
  • Confidentiality — We will cover data security in detail on Day 5, but for now: never paste sensitive client data into public AI tools
  • Fairness — Be aware that AI can reflect biases from its training data. Question assumptions in its output
  • Accountability — You are responsible for the work, not the AI. Your name goes on the report, not Claude's

The Terminal: A Gentle Introduction

What is a terminal?

A terminal (also called the "command line" or "shell") is a text-based interface for your computer. Instead of clicking icons and menus, you type commands. It looks like this:

Terminal $ _

That $ symbol is called the prompt — it is waiting for you to type a command. Do not be intimidated by it. You only need a handful of commands to use Claude Code effectively.

Tools, Permissions & How Claude Code Works

The permission model

Claude Code needs your permission before it does certain things. This is a safety feature. When Claude wants to read a file, write a file, or run a command, it will ask you first. You will see something like:

Claude wants to read the file: leases/tenant-abc-lease.pdf Allow? (y/n)

You can approve or deny each action. Over time, you can set up automatic approvals for common, safe actions.

Understanding tool use

Claude Code interacts with your computer using tools. Each tool does something specific:

Tool What it does Example use

Bash Runs a terminal command Processing data, running a script, checking file sizes
Grep Searches for text across files Finding every mention of "rent review" in a folder of leases

What Claude Code can and cannot access

Can access

  • Connected MCP services (Google, Xero, etc.)
  • The internet (for web searches, API calls)
  • Terminal commands on your machine

Cannot access

  • Files you have not given permission for
  • Other people's computers
  • Encrypted or password-protected files (without keys)
  • Actions blocked by your organisation's IT policy

MCP: How Claude Connects to External Services

What is MCP?

MCP stands for Model Context Protocol. It is the system that allows Claude Code to connect to external services like Google Workspace, Xero, Slack, and GitHub. Think of MCP as a set of power adapters — each one lets Claude plug into a different service.

Without MCP, Claude could only read files on your computer and chat with you. With MCP, Claude becomes a hub that can coordinate across all the tools your business uses.

Prompt Patterns & Common Mistakes

Prompts that work well for property tasks

The quality of Claude's output depends on the quality of your input. Here are patterns that produce the best results:

  • Be specific about the audience — "Write this for a client who is not familiar with property jargon" vs. "Write this for a fellow surveyor"
  • Specify the format — "Present this as a bullet-point summary" or "Create a table with columns for property, rent, and yield"
  • Give context — "This is for a rent review on an industrial unit. The current passing rent is £35,000 pa. The lease allows for upward-only reviews."
  • Set the tone — "Be formal and RICS-appropriate" or "Be concise and direct"
  • Ask for drafts, not finals — "Draft a first version of..." reminds both you and Claude that this needs review

Common mistakes to avoid

  • Being too vague — "Write something about the property" will produce generic output. Be specific about what you need.
  • Not providing context — Claude does not know about your specific client or property unless you tell it.
  • Trusting numbers blindly — Always verify any calculations, measurements, or financial figures Claude produces.
  • Skipping review — Never send AI output directly to a client. Always read it first.
  • Asking for professional opinions — Claude can draft, but the professional opinion must be yours.
  • Forgetting the knowledge cutoff — Do not ask Claude about very recent market transactions without providing the data.

What is Claude Co-work?

Beyond single conversations

On Day 1, you learned that Claude does not remember between conversations on claude.ai. Claude Co-work solves this problem. It allows Claude to maintain project knowledge across sessions, understand the context of your work, and collaborate on ongoing projects.

Think of it like this: on claude.ai, every conversation starts with a stranger. With Claude Co-work, Claude is a colleague who has read the project file and knows what has happened so far.

Shared context: CLAUDE.md files

At Hillway, every project has a file called CLAUDE.md. This is a set of instructions that Claude reads automatically at the start of each session. It contains:

  • Project status — What stage the project is at, what has been completed
  • Key decisions — Architecture choices, client preferences, important dates
  • How to work — Coding standards, naming conventions, team preferences
  • External connections — Which databases, APIs, and services are involved
  • Things to remember — Client quirks, past issues, lessons learned

This means when you start a Claude Code session in a project folder, Claude already knows about the project. You do not need to explain everything from scratch each time.

How co-work changes your workflow

Without co-work

  • Start every session by explaining context
  • Re-describe the project each time
  • Paste relevant documents again
  • Lose track of previous decisions

With co-work

  • Claude reads CLAUDE.md automatically
  • Remembers project state and decisions
  • Knows which files and services are relevant
  • Picks up where the last session left off

Agent Teams — Multiple Claudes Collaborating

What are agent teams?

An agent team is a group of Claude instances, each with a specific role, working together on a complex task. Instead of one Claude doing everything, you have specialists — like a real team.

Think of it like a property firm: you have a researcher, a report writer, a financial analyst, and a team lead. Each focuses on what they do best, and the team lead coordinates them. Agent teams work the same way.

How agent teams work

You give an instruction to the lead agent — "Prepare a rent review report for Unit 4, Industrial Park"

The lead agent breaks it down into tasks and assigns them to specialist agents

Specialist agents work in parallel — one researches comparables, one drafts the report, one checks the financials

Results are assembled by the lead agent into a coherent output

You review the final product and make adjustments

Writing Effective Prompts: 10 Principles

The art of prompting

The quality of AI output is directly proportional to the quality of your input. These 10 principles will help you get the best results from Claude every time.

1. Be specific, not vague

Weak: "Write about the property."
Strong: "Write a 500-word market overview for industrial units in Sheffield S9, focusing on rental values and vacancy rates in Q1 2026."

2. Provide context

Always tell Claude what it needs to know. Include the property details, client background, purpose of the document, and any constraints.

3. Specify the format

Tell Claude how you want the output structured: bullet points, table, narrative paragraphs, numbered list, comparison, or a specific template.

4. Define the audience

"Write this for a property fund manager" produces very different output from "Write this for a first-time landlord with no property experience."

5. Set the tone

Formal, conversational, technical, friendly, concise, detailed — tell Claude what register to use. "RICS-appropriate and formal" is a good default for professional work.

6. Use examples

If you want output in a specific style, show Claude an example. "Write in the same style as this: [paste example]" is extremely effective.

7. Break complex tasks into steps

Instead of "Create a complete rent review report", try: "First, summarise the lease terms. Then, list the comparable evidence. Then, analyse the evidence and recommend a revised rent."

8. Ask for drafts, iterate

"Draft a first version" sets the expectation that you will refine. After the first output, say: "Good start. Now make it more concise" or "Add more detail to section 3."

9. Constrain the output

Set boundaries: "Keep this under 200 words", "Use no more than 5 bullet points", "Do not include legal advice." Constraints improve focus.

10. Verify and challenge

After receiving output, ask: "Are you confident about the figures in this analysis?" or "What assumptions did you make?" Claude will be transparent about uncertainty.

RICS Standards, Professional Judgement & When NOT to Use AI

The RICS position on AI

RICS recognises that AI tools are increasingly used in surveying practice but emphasises that:

  • AI is a tool to support professional judgement, not replace it
  • The regulated professional remains responsible for all outputs
  • Firms must ensure appropriate oversight and quality control
  • Transparency with clients about AI use is expected
  • Data protection and confidentiality must be maintained

Decision framework: AI vs professional judgement

Task Use AI? Why
Drafting an email Yes (with review) AI excels at drafting text; you review for accuracy and tone
Summarising a lease Yes (with verification) AI can extract terms quickly; you verify against the original
Providing a formal valuation No Requires professional judgement, local knowledge, and RICS qualification
Organising comparable evidence Yes AI can sort, filter, and present data efficiently
Advising on a dispute No Requires professional judgement and potentially legal expertise
Calculating service charges Yes (with review) AI can process numbers; you verify the methodology and figures
Site inspection No Requires physical presence and professional observation
Formatting inspection notes Yes AI can structure your raw notes into a professional format

The key question is not "can AI do this?" but "should AI do this without professional oversight?" If the answer involves professional judgement, opinion, or physical observation, the answer is always: human first, AI assists.

Data Security & Spotting AI Failures

Data security rules at Hillway

These rules are non-negotiable:

  • Never share client personal data (names, addresses, financial details) with public AI tools (free ChatGPT, etc.)
  • Claude Code is approved for use with client data because it runs locally and connects via authenticated MCP servers
  • Never paste passwords, API keys, or security credentials into any AI tool
  • Never upload confidential documents to public AI services
  • Be careful with commercially sensitive information (ongoing negotiations, unreleased valuations, deal terms)
  • When in doubt, ask Matt before sharing data with any AI system

Common AI failures and how to spot them

  • Hallucinated facts — Claude may cite case law, legislation, or statistics that do not exist. Always verify citations.
  • Confident mistakes — AI does not say "I am not sure" often enough. If a figure seems too neat or an argument too convenient, double-check.
  • Outdated information — AI's training data has a cutoff. Recent legislation changes, market data, or events may not be reflected.
  • Pattern-matching errors — AI may apply general patterns incorrectly to specific situations (e.g., applying residential norms to commercial property).
  • Missing nuance — Local market knowledge, personal relationships, and political context are invisible to AI.
  • Calculation errors — While less common, AI can make arithmetic mistakes. Always verify numbers independently.

The review-edit-verify workflow

Review — Read the AI output carefully. Does it make sense? Does the tone match your expectations?

Edit — Adjust wording, add your professional insight, correct any errors, and add information the AI missed.

Verify — Cross-check all facts, figures, dates, and citations against original sources. Run calculations independently.

Sign off — Only release the work once you are satisfied it meets your professional standards.
